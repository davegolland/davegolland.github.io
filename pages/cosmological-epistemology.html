How We Know

The universe is 13.8 billion years old. We've been doing science for about 400 years. How can we claim to know what happened in the first three minutes — and why should anyone believe us?

Chapter I

The Problem of Deep Time

How do you know anything about events no one was present to witness?

We claim detailed, quantitative knowledge about events 13.8 billion years ago that lasted fractions of a second and will never happen again. No one was there. Nothing recorded them. They can't be repeated.

A chemist can rerun an experiment. A cosmologist cannot rerun the Big Bang. So what grounds do we have for confidence?

Better grounds than you'd expect. The early universe didn't leave a diary, but it left physical traces — still measurable today — that must be consistent with each other if any story about the past is true.

The universe is not a text to be interpreted. It is a physical system whose past is overdetermined by what we can measure today. — The central claim of physical cosmology

The word to hold onto is overdetermined. We don't have one clue — we have many, from different physics, measured by different instruments. And they agree. That agreement is the source of confidence.

Chapter II

The Ladder of Evidence

Not all evidence is created equal. A taxonomy of how we know.

Cosmological knowledge rests on four types of evidence, each carrying different weight. Think of them as rungs — lower is more certain, higher is more speculative.

More speculative
Theoretical Framework

Mathematically plausible

The theory is self-consistent and solves known problems, but lacks observational confirmation.

String theory, loop quantum gravity — beautiful mathematics, but no confirmed predictions about anything observable. The Planck era (before 10⁻⁴³ seconds) lives entirely here. We have frameworks, not knowledge.

Indirect Inference

Consistent with, but not unique

The observations are what we'd expect if the theory is true, but other explanations might also fit.

Inflation is the classic example. The CMB's flatness and large-scale correlations are consistent with inflation — but "consistent with" is weaker than "proven by." The observations don't uniquely demand inflation. This is a real distinction that cosmologists respect, even when popular accounts blur it.

Lab-Calibrated Physics

Same physics, tested here

Nuclear reactions, atomic transitions, particle interactions — all tested in terrestrial labs, then applied to early-universe conditions.

Nucleosynthesis uses nuclear cross-sections measured in labs. The atomic physics of recombination is the same physics as fluorescent lights. We're not guessing — we're applying well-tested laws in a new regime. The risk is that the laws might differ at extreme energies, but at nucleosynthesis scales (~MeV), they're thoroughly validated.

click to expand
Direct Observation

We see it happening

Light or particles from the event reach our instruments. The CMB is the premier example — actual photons from 380,000 years after the Big Bang.

The CMB isn't an inference — it's light that has been traveling for 13.8 billion years and lands on our detectors. Galaxy surveys are also direct: we map millions of galaxies and measure their patterns. The catch: we can only observe events that left signals that survive to the present.

The critical skill is knowing which rung you're on. Public communication often fails here, treating inflation with the same confidence as nucleosynthesis. The gradient is steep. Acknowledging it makes the whole framework more credible.

Chapter III

The CMB: A Photograph of the Past

How one dataset encodes an extraordinary amount of information.

380,000 years after the Big Bang, the universe cooled enough for electrons to bind to protons. It went from opaque plasma to transparent gas. The light released at that moment has been traveling ever since, stretched by expansion from visible light down to microwaves.

We call it the Cosmic Microwave Background. It fills the sky at 2.725 K, uniform in every direction to one part in 100,000.

But those tiny fluctuations are where the information lives.

The Power Spectrum

Break the CMB temperature map into spherical harmonics — essentially a Fourier transform on a sphere. Plot fluctuation amplitude against angular scale. You get a curve with distinctive peaks: the CMB power spectrum.

CMB Angular Power Spectrum (Simplified)

Each peak encodes different physics. Peak 1: geometry (the universe is flat). Peak 2: baryon density. Peak 3: dark matter density. Relative heights: baryon-to-photon ratio. Damping tail: when the universe became transparent.

The positions, heights, and widths of these peaks are predicted by a model with just six free parameters. The data contains thousands of independent measurements. Six knobs, thousands of constraints.

"Over-constrained" means: if you have as many parameters as data points, any model can fit. But six parameters fitting thousands of points means the model is predicting most of the data — and getting it right. That's very hard to fake.

Analogy: someone claims to know a cake recipe. Predicting one fact about the cake is weak evidence. Predicting 2,500 — density, color, crumb structure, moisture, rise height, flavor — from six ingredients, and getting them all right? The recipe is almost certainly correct.

Chapter IV

Concordance: When Independent Lines Agree

The deepest argument in cosmological epistemology is the pattern of agreement across measurements that have no business agreeing, unless the underlying story is true.

Consider a single number: the baryon density of the universe — how much ordinary matter exists per cubic meter, on average. We can measure this three completely different ways:

Three measurements. Three different physics. Three different instruments. Three different eras of the universe. Same answer to within 1%. The probability of this being coincidental is vanishingly small.

This is called concordance, and it's the backbone of modern cosmology. Any single measurement can be wrong — systematic errors, calibration mistakes, hidden biases. But when independent measurements, using different physics, by different teams, on different observations, all land on the same answer — the simplest explanation is that the answer is right.

Independent convergence is the closest thing science has to proof. Each sightline alone is uncertain. Their intersection is sharp.

The same logic convicts in court and diagnoses in medicine. Cosmology is an unusually clean case; the measurements are so independent that shared errors are implausible.

Chapter V

Extrapolation and Its Limits

When can we trust lab-tested physics applied to conditions we'll never recreate?

Much of what we know rests on a single move: take physics tested in labs and apply it to conditions far more extreme than any lab can achieve. When is this justified?

It depends on how far you're reaching beyond tested ground.

Nucleosynthesis ~3 minutes Nuclear physics — thoroughly tested in labs
Electroweak ~10⁻¹² s Partially tested at LHC
GUT / Inflation ~10⁻³⁶ s No direct lab tests possible
Planck ~10⁻⁴³ s No known theory

Nucleosynthesis (~MeV): The nuclear reactions have been measured in accelerators. Extreme temperatures, familiar physics. Short reach. High confidence.

Electroweak era (~TeV): The LHC reaches these energies. We've confirmed the Higgs. But the Standard Model works here while being known to be incomplete. Moderate reach. Moderate confidence.

Inflation (~10¹⁶ GeV): A trillion times beyond the LHC. We have theoretical frameworks and indirect evidence, but no direct test. Enormous reach. Low confidence.

Planck era (~10¹⁹ GeV): General relativity and quantum mechanics are both needed and they contradict each other. We don't even know which theory to extrapolate from. Undefined reach. Near-zero confidence.

Key Epistemological Principle

Confidence degrades gracefully. There's no sharp line between "known" and "unknown," only a gradient. Beware anyone who presents the entire timeline with uniform confidence.

Chapter VI

The Role of Prediction

Predicting what you haven't seen yet is the real test of a theory.

There's an old distinction in philosophy of science: accommodation (building a theory to fit data you already have) versus prediction (using a theory to forecast data you haven't yet seen). Both matter, but prediction carries more weight. It rules out the possibility that the theory was quietly tuned to fit.

Cosmology has an impressive track record of novel predictions:

Predicted What Confirmed Status
1948 Relic radiation from the hot early universe should fill all of space at ~5 K 1965 ✓ Measured at 2.725 K
1948 Primordial helium abundance should be ~25% by mass 1970s ✓ Measured at 24.7%
1970 CMB should have a perfect blackbody spectrum 1990 ✓ COBE: most perfect blackbody ever measured
1970 CMB should show acoustic peaks at specific angular scales 2000 ✓ BOOMERanG, then WMAP, then Planck
1998 Galaxy distributions should show a "standard ruler" at 490 million light-years 2005 ✓ SDSS baryon acoustic oscillation detection
1980 Inflation predicts primordial gravitational wave imprint in CMB polarization — ○ Not yet detected

These predictions came decades before confirmation. The CMB temperature was off by a factor of two (5 K vs. 2.7 K) — which sounds imprecise until you realize the real prediction was that any such radiation should exist at all, filling all of space. That turned out to be right.

Notice the last row. Inflation predicts a gravitational-wave signature we haven't found yet. The theory has stuck its neck out. If the observation eventually contradicts it, that version of inflation is falsified. That's how this is supposed to work.

Why Predictions Matter More Than Explanations

A theory that predicts new data had to get the answer right before seeing it. Explaining existing data might be a just-so story. The more ways a prediction could have gone wrong, the stronger the confirmation when it doesn't.

Chapter VII

What We Don't Know

Confidence in what we know coexists honestly with major open questions.

Cosmology is not a finished story. Several deep problems remain open, and they coexist comfortably with what we do know.

Dark Matter

~27% of the universe's energy density. We know its gravitational effects precisely. We have no idea what it's made of. No particle candidate has been confirmed despite decades of experiments.

Dark Energy

~68% of the universe's energy density. Drives accelerating expansion. Could be a cosmological constant, a dynamic field, or a sign that general relativity is wrong at large scales. We genuinely don't know.

The Inflation Mechanism

Inflation solves several problems elegantly, but we don't know what physical field drove it, why it started, or why it stopped. There are hundreds of proposed models. No observation yet distinguishes between them.

The Hubble Tension

Two methods of measuring the expansion rate give persistently different answers: 67.4 (CMB) vs. 73.0 (local distance ladder) km/s/Mpc. Either one measurement has a systematic error, or there's new physics we're missing.

Quantum Gravity

General relativity and quantum mechanics are both correct in their domains but incompatible. At the Planck scale (10⁻⁴³ seconds), we need both and have neither. The origin of the universe is behind this wall.

Matter-Antimatter Asymmetry

The universe contains matter but essentially no antimatter. The Standard Model can't explain the imbalance. Something happened in the first microseconds that we don't understand.

A naive view says: if you don't know everything, you don't know anything. But we know the baryon density to 1% and we don't know what dark matter is. These aren't in tension. Knowledge is granular. The maturity of a field shows in how clearly it distinguishes what it knows from what it doesn't.

Chapter VIII

Lessons for Epistemology

What cosmology teaches us about the nature of knowledge itself.

These lessons apply far outside physics.

Lesson 1 — Independent Convergence is the Gold Standard

Any single piece of evidence can be doubted. But when independent lines, different methods, different physics, converge on the same answer, the chance they're all wrong in the same way becomes negligible. This works in medicine and law. Cosmology is an unusually clean case.

Lesson 2 — Quantitative Predictions Beat Qualitative Stories

"The universe started hot and cooled down" is a story. "The primordial helium fraction should be 24.7 ± 0.1% by mass" is a prediction. The difference: the prediction can be wrong. Theories that make precise, testable claims — and survive — earn more credence than theories that only tell plausible stories.

Lesson 3 — Confidence Degrades Gracefully

Knowledge isn't binary. "We know X" vs. "we don't" is almost always the wrong frame. Better: "How confident, and why?" In cosmology, confidence runs smoothly from ~99.99% (CMB blackbody) through ~95% (baryon density) to ~50% (inflation details) to ~0% (Planck era). Every field should be this honest about its gradient.

Lesson 4 — Simplicity is Evidence

Six parameters. Fits the CMB, element abundances, galaxy clustering, supernovae, gravitational lensing. A model this simple working this broadly is itself evidence that it's capturing something real. A wrong model would need patches, corrections, special cases. The absence of patches is informative.

Lesson 5 — Honest Uncertainty Builds Credibility

Cosmology's credibility comes partly from saying "we don't know" about dark matter, dark energy, and quantum gravity, while defending what it does know. A field that claimed certainty about everything would be less trustworthy. Acknowledging boundaries is what makes the claims within them believable.

The universe left receipts. They're internally consistent. Where measurements disagree, where theories break down, where the data runs out, we say so. That is the method working.
