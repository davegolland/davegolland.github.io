PretrainRL: Alleviating Factuality Hallucination of Large Language Models at the Beginning

Source: arXiv:cs.CL Published: 2026-02-02 Rank: #4 (score: 0.99, max_sim: 0.82) Reading time: ~20 min Tags: RL, LLM, RAG Code: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2602.01875v1

Abstract

Large language models (LLMs), despite their powerful capabilities, suffer from factual hallucinations where they generate verifiable falsehoods. We identify a root of this issue: the imbalanced data distribution in the pretraining corpus, which leads to a state of “low-probability truth” and “high-probability falsehood”. Recent approaches, such as teaching models to say “I don’t know” or post-hoc knowledge editing, either evade the problem or face catastrophic forgetting. To address this issue from its root, we propose \textbf{PretrainRL}, a novel framework that integrates reinforcement learning into the pretraining phase to consolidate factual knowledge. The core principle of PretrainRL is “\textbf{debiasing then learning}.” It actively reshapes the model’s probability distribution by down-weighting high-probability falsehoods, thereby making “room” for low-probability truths to be learned effectively. To enable this, we design an efficient negative sampling strategy to discover these high-probability falsehoods and introduce novel metrics to evaluate the model’s probabilistic state concerning factual knowledge. Extensive experiments on three public benchmarks demonstrate that PretrainRL significantly alleviates factual hallucinations and outperforms state-of-the-art methods.

Key Techniques

  • Debiasing then learning
  • Efficient negative sampling strategy
  • Novel metrics for probabilistic state evaluation

Efficient Negative Sampling Strategy

Core Idea: A targeted sampling mechanism to discover high-probability falsehoods in the pretraining corpus—statements the model assigns high likelihood but are factually incorrect—enabling efficient RL-based suppression.

Problem It Solves: Naively sampling all false statements is computationally prohibitive. The strategy must efficiently identify the most impactful falsehoods (high model probability + factually wrong) to maximize training signal with limited annotation budget.

How It Works:

  1. Model probability estimation: For candidate statements in the corpus, compute the model’s predicted probability P(statement | context) before intervention.
  2. Factuality filtering: Filter candidates to retain only those with ground-truth label = false (using available factuality annotations, crowdsourcing, or fact-checking databases).
  3. Ranking by impact: Sort filtered falsehoods by their model-assigned probability in descending order, selecting top-k for negative supervision signals during RL.
  4. Dynamic updating: As the model improves through RL iterations, re-sample high-probability falsehoods to maintain training difficulty and relevance.

Where It’s Useful:

  • Constructing training signals for factuality-focused RL during pretraining
  • Budget-constrained scenarios where not all data can be annotated
  • Curriculum learning approaches that gradually increase training difficulty
  • Identifying model weaknesses for targeted fine-tuning

Limitations / Caveats:

  • Relies on availability of ground-truth factuality labels, which may be sparse or subjective
  • High-probability falsehoods may be statistically rare, making efficient sampling challenging
  • Sampling bias: model-based selection may miss falsehoods the model is confident about for the wrong reasons
  • Computational cost of probability estimation scales with corpus size

Internal Use Cases:

  • Negative sampling in recommendation ranking to down-weight low-quality items the ranker incorrectly scores high
  • Curriculum learning strategies that prioritize hard negatives for collaborative filtering or ranking models

Probabilistic Factuality Metrics

Core Idea: Novel metrics to evaluate a model’s probabilistic state regarding factual knowledge—measuring the distributional alignment between true and false statements to quantify hallucination risk.

Problem It Solves: Existing evaluation focuses on end-to-end accuracy (does the model generate the right answer?) but misses the probabilistic condition underlying hallucinations. Metrics are needed to assess whether the model has resolved the “low-probability truth” / “high-probability falsehood” imbalance.

How It Works:

  1. Compute probability distributions: For each true fact and corresponding false counterstatement, measure the model’s predicted probability: P(true_statement) and P(false_statement).
  2. Probability margin calculation: Calculate the margin as P(true) - P(false) across test cases; positive margins indicate correct ordering.
  3. Aggregate statistics: Compute metrics such as:
    • Truth probability gap: Mean P(true) - Mean P(false) across all test pairs
    • Margin consistency: Fraction of test cases where P(true) > P(false)
    • Entropy/calibration: Measure of how concentrated probability mass is on truths vs. falsehoods
  4. Benchmarking: Use these metrics alongside accuracy to assess whether hallucination risk has been reduced even if downstream task accuracy plateaus.

Where It’s Useful:

  • Evaluating intermediate states during pretraining without waiting for end-to-end task evaluation
  • Diagnosing models that generate correct answers by luck but have poor probabilistic calibration
  • Monitoring fine-tuned models to ensure they retain factual robustness
  • Comparing different debiasing or knowledge editing interventions

Limitations / Caveats:

  • Requires paired true/false statements for evaluation—labor-intensive to construct comprehensive test sets
  • Metrics are sensitive to how “false” counterstatements are chosen (adversarial negatives may not exist)
  • May not capture all forms of hallucinations (e.g., internally consistent but globally false narratives)
  • Metrics can be gamed if not carefully designed (model may learn to suppress falsehoods without learning truths)

Internal Use Cases:

  • Ranking systems could use analogous probability margin metrics to ensure top-k items are not only predicted high but are actually relevant
  • Item quality prediction models could track “true positive probability” vs. “false positive probability” to monitor calibration

Analysis of PretrainRL in Context of Prior Work

PretrainRL fundamentally challenges a core assumption underlying the matched work on Early-Exit Large Language Models. While early-exit methods optimize efficiency by leveraging confident predictions at intermediate layers, PretrainRL identifies a deeper problem: the model’s probability distributions themselves are miscalibrated regarding factuality. The early-exit work assumes that high-confidence predictions are reliable, but PretrainRL reveals that models can be highly confident about falsehoods due to imbalanced pretraining data. This suggests that early-exit systems built on uncalibrated models may propagate hallucinations efficiently—a critical blind spot in the efficiency-focused approach. PretrainRL’s “debiasing then learning” principle would need to precede early-exit optimization to ensure that intermediate-layer confidence is actually trustworthy.

The connection to Lengthy Context Retrieval via LLM Pretraining is more subtle but important. That work leverages large pre-training context windows to enable better retrieval generalization, implicitly assuming that larger, more diverse pretraining corpora improve downstream performance. PretrainRL complicates this narrative by showing that simply scaling pretraining data without addressing distributional imbalance—where falsehoods may occur more frequently than truths in certain domains—actually worsens factuality. The paper’s negative sampling strategy to identify high-probability falsehoods represents a qualitative shift: rather than passively inheriting whatever distributions exist in raw corpora, PretrainRL actively reshapes the probability landscape during pretraining. This has implications for how we think about pretraining corpus composition and quality, suggesting that future work on context-aware pretraining should incorporate factuality-aware debiasing.

PretrainRL introduces methodological novelty that bridges to both prior works: it reframes pretraining not as a fixed statistical task but as an active reshaping problem. While early-exit methods optimize computation and context-retrieval work optimizes generalization, PretrainRL optimizes the probabilistic calibration of factual knowledge itself. The novel metrics for evaluating the model’s probabilistic state concerning factual knowledge suggest that future pretraining approaches should monitor and correct distributional biases continuously, rather than treating the model’s learned probability distribution as an inevitable byproduct of data. This positions PretrainRL as foundational work that should precede downstream optimizations like early-exiting or context-aware retrieval.
