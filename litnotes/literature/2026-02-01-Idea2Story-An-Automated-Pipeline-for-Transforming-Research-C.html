Abstract

Autonomous scientific discovery with large language model (LLM)-based agents has recently made substantial progress, demonstrating the ability to automate end-to-end research workflows. However, existing systems largely rely on runtime-centric execution paradigms, repeatedly reading, summarizing, and reasoning over large volumes of scientific literature online. This on-the-spot computation strategy incurs high computational cost, suffers from context window limitations, and often leads to brittle reasoning and hallucination. We propose Idea2Story, a pre-computation-driven framework for autonomous scientific discovery that shifts literature understanding from online reasoning to offline knowledge construction. Idea2Story continuously collects peer-reviewed papers together with their review feedback, extracts core methodological units, composes reusable research patterns, and organizes them into a structured methodological knowledge graph. At runtime, underspecified user research intents are aligned to established research paradigms, enabling efficient retrieval and reuse of high-quality research patterns instead of open-ended generation and trial-and-error. By grounding research planning and execution in a pre-built knowledge graph, Idea2Story alleviates the context window bottleneck of LLMs and substantially reduces repeated runtime reasoning over literature. We conduct qualitative analyses and preliminary empirical studies demonstrating that Idea2Story can generate coherent, methodologically grounded, and novel research patterns, and can produce several high-quality research demonstrations in an end-to-end setting. These results suggest that offline knowledge construction provides a practical and scalable foundation for reliable autonomous scientific discovery.

Intent-to-Paradigm Alignment

Core Idea: A runtime mechanism that maps underspecified user research intents to established research paradigms stored in the knowledge graph, enabling pattern matching and retrieval rather than open-ended generation.

Problem It Solves:

  • Open-ended research planning leads to hallucinated or invalid methodological sequences
  • Underspecified user queries lack sufficient context for direct execution
  • LLMs generate arbitrary solutions without grounding in proven research patterns
  • Context window limitations prevent comprehensive literature search at runtime

How It Works:

  1. Intent Parsing: Process user-provided research intent (which may be incomplete or conceptual)
  2. Semantic Encoding: Encode the intent into a vector representation capturing key research dimensions (domain, objective, constraints)
  3. Paradigm Matching: Query the pre-built knowledge graph to identify compatible research paradigms using similarity matching or graph traversal
  4. Ranking: Rank candidate paradigms by fit quality (e.g., methodological soundness, novelty score, review sentiment from training papers)
  5. Alignment: Select the best-matching paradigm(s) and resolve ambiguities in the user intent through paradigm structure

Where It’s Useful:

  • Research proposal generation where user provides high-level research questions
  • Automated literature-informed experimental design
  • Intelligent research assistant systems
  • Grant proposal scaffolding with methodological validation

Limitations / Caveats:

  • Relies on existence of similar paradigms in the knowledge graph; novel research directions may not align well
  • Quality of alignment depends on quality of semantic encoding and paradigm representation
  • May under-explore truly innovative methodological combinations by defaulting to nearest established paradigm
  • Requires effective handling of ambiguous or conflicting intents

Internal Use Cases:

  • Similar to query-to-document matching in ranking systems but at the methodological pattern level
  • Could use learned embeddings for intent-paradigm similarity scoring (e.g., as a filtering or re-ranking stage)
  • Applicable to cold-start problems in recommendation: matching new research ideas to proven patterns is analogous to matching users to similar cohorts

Methodological Pattern Composition & Validation

Core Idea: An extraction and synthesis process that identifies reusable methodological patterns from papers and their peer reviews, ensuring patterns are validated by the research community before inclusion in the knowledge graph.

Problem It Solves:

  • Raw papers contain methods buried in text and context-dependent descriptions
  • No explicit ranking or validation of methodological components
  • Methodological gaps and flawed approaches aren’t automatically filtered out
  • No structured encoding of what makes a method effective (review feedback signal)

How It Works:

  1. Unit Extraction: Identify methodological components from paper text (e.g., specific algorithms, data preprocessing steps, evaluation protocols)
  2. Context Extraction: Capture conditions under which each component is applied (e.g., dataset characteristics, baseline comparisons)
  3. Review Feedback Integration: Extract relevant critique and validation signals from peer reviews for each methodological unit
  4. Pattern Generalization: Identify recurring methodological sequences across multiple papers
  5. Validation Scoring: Assign quality/reliability scores based on: citation counts of source papers, sentiment from peer reviews, breadth of replication across papers
  6. Graph Integration: Add validated patterns as nodes to the knowledge graph with edges encoding sequencing and compatibility constraints

Where It’s Useful:

  • Building methodologically validated knowledge bases for specific research domains
  • Identifying best practices and standard protocols in scientific fields
  • Creating audit trails for methodological decisions in generated research
  • Detecting methodological novelty by identifying patterns not yet well-established

Limitations / Caveats:

  • Extracting methodological units requires domain knowledge or sophisticated NLP; fully automatic extraction may be lossy
  • Review feedback is heterogeneous in quality and may reflect reviewer bias rather than true methodological validity
  • Validation signals (citations, review sentiment) are historical and may not predict success of novel combinations
  • Generalization across papers can obscure important context-dependencies in method application

Internal Use Cases:

  • For ranking models: methodological validation score could serve as a quality signal in multi-stage ranking pipelines
  • For RecSys: validation patterns could inform compatibility constraints in composition-based recommendation (e.g., “method A works well with preprocessing B”)
  • Could enable counterfactual scoring where estimated quality of research patterns informs downstream ranking decisions
