GOSV: Global Optimization for Safety Vector Extraction in LLMs

GOSV: Global Optimization for Safety Vector Extraction in LLMs

Source: arXiv:2601.15801 (cs.LG) Date: 2026-01-22 Score: 2.56 (max_sim=0.744, novel interpretability angle)

The Problem

LLM safety guardrails are fragile against jailbreaks. Existing interpretability methods use local, greedy attribution — they assume components (attention heads) contribute independently. But safety is cooperative: multiple heads work together.

Key Insight

Safety behavior isn’t controlled by individual heads — it emerges from global coordination across many heads. To find the true safety-critical components, you need to optimize over all heads simultaneously.

The GOSV Framework

Global Optimization Approach

Instead of testing heads one-by-one (greedy), GOSV finds the optimal set of heads to modify using global optimization.

Two Complementary Strategies

1. Harmful Patching

  • Inject activations from harmful prompt processing
  • Identifies “Malicious Injection Vectors” — heads that, when activated, enable harmful outputs

2. Zero Ablation

  • Zero out head activations
  • Identifies “Safety Suppression Vectors” — heads that, when disabled, break safety

Key Finding: Two Distinct Pathways

The two strategies find spatially distinct head sets with low overlap. This means:

Aligned LLMs maintain separate functional pathways for safety — one for detecting harmful content, another for suppressing harmful outputs.

Quantitative Results

  • ~30% of total heads need repatching for complete safety breakdown
  • Consistent across multiple model architectures
  • Enables a novel white-box jailbreak that outperforms existing attacks

Why This Matters

For LLM Safety Research

  • Provides mechanistic understanding of how safety works
  • Identifies specific components to monitor/protect
  • Dual-pathway finding suggests redundancy in safety design

For Adversarial ML

  • New attack surface: activation patching is more effective than prompt-based jailbreaks
  • White-box attacks remain a threat even for aligned models

Broader Implications

If safety is distributed across ~30% of heads, then:

  1. Pruning/quantization must be careful about which heads to remove
  2. Fine-tuning can inadvertently break safety by modifying these heads
  3. Safety monitoring could track activations in critical heads

Key Technique to Extract

Global optimization over component sets — instead of greedy/local attribution, search the joint space of component combinations. More expensive but finds cooperative effects.

Questions

  1. Do these safety vectors transfer across model families?
  2. Could similar analysis find “recommendation safety” pathways (e.g., preventing filter bubbles)?
  3. What’s the relationship between safety vectors and RLHF training dynamics?
