Asymmetric Prompt Weighting for Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards

Source: arXiv:cs.LG Published: 2026-02-11 Rank: #7 (score: 0.97, max_sim: 0.83) Reading time: ~20 min Tags: RL, LLM

Abstract

Reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards has driven recent advances in LLM post-training, in particular for reasoning. Policy optimization algorithms generate a number of responses for a given prompt and then effectively weight the corresponding gradients depending on the rewards. The most popular algorithms including GRPO, DAPO, and RLOO focus on ambiguous prompts, i.e., prompts with intermediate success probability, while downgrading gradients with very easy and very hard prompts. In this paper, we consider asymmetric prompt weightings that assign higher weights to prompts with low, or even zero, empirical success probability. We find that asymmetric weighting particularly benefits from-scratch RL (as in R1-Zero), where training traverses a wide accuracy range, and less so in post-SFT RL where the model already starts at high accuracy. We also provide theory that characterizes prompt weights which minimize the time needed to raise success probability from an initial level to a target accuracy under a fixed update budget. In low-success regimes, where informative responses are rare and response cost dominates, these optimal weights become asymmetric, upweighting low success probabilities and thereby accelerating effective-time convergence.

Asymmetric Prompt Weighting (APW)

Core Idea: Weight policy gradient updates asymmetrically by assigning higher importance to prompts with low or zero empirical success probability, accelerating learning in low-success regimes where informative responses are rare.

Problem It Solves: Existing RL algorithms (GRPO, DAPO, RLOO) focus on ambiguous prompts with intermediate success probability, downweighting very hard prompts. This slows convergence in from-scratch RL (e.g., R1-Zero) where models start with low accuracy and must efficiently learn from rare correct responses.

How It Works:

  1. Sample responses: Generate multiple responses per prompt and compute verifiable rewards (e.g., correctness on math/reasoning tasks)
  2. Estimate success probability: Calculate empirical success rate p̂(prompt) from the sampled responses
  3. Apply asymmetric weighting: Assign higher gradient weights to prompts with low p̂, inverting the typical symmetric weighting that favors p̂ ≈ 0.5
  4. Update policy: Apply weighted policy gradients, prioritizing learning signal from hard prompts where correct responses are most informative

Architecture:

flowchart LR
    A[Prompt] --> B[Sample N responses]
    B --> C[Compute rewards]
    C --> D[Estimate p̂ success]
    D --> E[Asymmetric weight w p̂]
    E --> F[Weighted gradient update]
    F --> G[Updated policy]

Where It’s Useful:

  • From-scratch RL training (R1-Zero style) where model traverses wide accuracy ranges from near-zero to high performance
  • Reasoning tasks (math, coding) with verifiable binary rewards where hard problems provide crucial learning signal
  • Cold-start scenarios where initial model competence is low and symmetric weighting wastes compute on already-solved easy prompts

Limitations / Caveats:

  • Less beneficial for post-SFT RL where models already start at high accuracy (most prompts already have high p̂)
  • Requires verifiable rewards (correct/incorrect) - doesn’t apply to subjective preference optimization
  • Theoretical optimality assumes low-success regime; may need adaptive weighting schedules as training progresses
  • Increased variance in gradient estimates from rare successes may require larger batch sizes or careful tuning

Internal Use Cases:

  • Cold-start LLM reasoning fine-tuning: When deploying R1-style RL from weak base models, prioritize learning from hardest problems first
  • Curriculum learning for RecSys: Analogously weight hard-to-rank queries more heavily when training ranking models
  • Active learning: Use asymmetric weighting principles to prioritize labeling budget on low-confidence examples where model signal is weakest
