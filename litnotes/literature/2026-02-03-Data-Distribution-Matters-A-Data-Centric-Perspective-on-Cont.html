Data Distribution Matters: A Data-Centric Perspective on Context Compression for Large Language Model

Data Distribution Matters: A Data-Centric Perspective on Context Compression for Large Language Model

Source: arXiv:cs.CL Published: 2026-02-02 Rank: #8 (score: 0.97, max_sim: 0.84) Reading time: ~0 min Tags: Uncategorized Code: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2602.01778v1

Abstract

The deployment of Large Language Models (LLMs) in long-context scenarios is hindered by computational inefficiency and significant information redundancy. Although recent advancements have widely adopted context compression to address these challenges, existing research only focus on model-side improvements, the impact of the data distribution itself on context compression remains largely unexplored. To bridge this gap, we are the first to adopt a data-centric perspective to systematically investigate how data distribution impacts compression quality, including two dimensions: input data and intrinsic data (i.e., the model’s internal pretrained knowledge). We evaluate the semantic integrity of compressed representations using an autoencoder-based framework to systematically investigate it. Our experimental results reveal that: (1) encoder-measured input entropy negatively correlates with compression quality, while decoder-measured entropy shows no significant relationship under a frozen-decoder setting; and (2) the gap between intrinsic data of the encoder and decoder significantly diminishes compression gains, which is hard to mitigate. Based on these findings, we further present practical guidelines to optimize compression gains.

Encoder-Decoder Entropy Gap Analysis for Compression Quality Prediction

Core Idea: The discrepancy between encoder-measured entropy and decoder-measured entropy acts as a principled predictor of compression quality degradation, where larger gaps indicate harder-to-mitigate compression losses.

Problem It Solves: Context compression quality varies unpredictably across datasets because the model’s pretrained knowledge (decoder) may not align with the actual input data distribution (encoder). This misalignment is a fundamental bottleneck that cannot be solved by improving the compression method alone.

How It Works:

  1. Encoder Entropy Quantification: Measure H_enc as the Shannon entropy of the input context distribution: H_enc = -Σ p(x) log p(x), where p(x) is the probability distribution of input tokens/contexts
  2. Decoder Entropy Quantification: Measure H_dec as the entropy of the frozen decoder’s internal knowledge representation, reflecting what knowledge the model can reliably reconstruct
  3. Gap Calculation: Compute ΔH = H_enc - H_dec as the entropy divergence metric
  4. Quality Correlation: Empirically establish that larger ΔH values correlate with lower compression quality (task performance), independent of compression method used
  5. Mitigation Assessment: Attempt to reduce ΔH through fine-tuning or knowledge injection; measure how much compression quality improves when ΔH shrinks

Where It’s Useful:

  • Feasibility assessment: Predicting whether a compression strategy will work before deployment
  • Dataset selection: Choosing datasets likely to benefit from compression in training/evaluation
  • Resource allocation: Prioritizing computational investment in compression for high-ΔH problems versus accepting lower compression ratios
  • Cross-domain generalization: Identifying when models need domain-specific fine-tuning before compression

Limitations / Caveats:

  • The entropy gap “is hard to mitigate” — even with fine-tuning or knowledge injection, fundamental misalignment persists
  • Entropy is a coarse-grained metric; two distributions with equal entropy may have different compression properties
  • Does not identify what specific knowledge is missing; only that a gap exists
  • The relationship between ΔH and compression quality may be non-linear or dataset-dependent

Internal Use Cases:

  • LLM ranking models: Predict whether context compression will hurt ranking quality for different query-document distribution pairs
  • RecSys systems: Assess whether user history compression (e.g., sequence truncation) will lose important behavioral signals given user diversity (high H_enc) vs. model capacity (H_dec)
  • Multi-task learning: Identify which tasks have high encoder-decoder misalignment and prioritize them for task-specific fine-tuning before shared compression
