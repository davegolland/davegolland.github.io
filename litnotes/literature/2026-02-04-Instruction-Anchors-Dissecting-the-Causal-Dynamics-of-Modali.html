Instruction Anchors: Dissecting the Causal Dynamics of Modality Arbitration

Source: arXiv:cs.CL Published: 2026-02-03 Rank: #3 (score: 1.00, max_sim: 0.82) Reading time: ~20 min Tags: LLM Code: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2602.03677v1

Abstract

Modality following serves as the capacity of multimodal large language models (MLLMs) to selectively utilize multimodal contexts based on user instructions. It is fundamental to ensuring safety and reliability in real-world deployments. However, the underlying mechanisms governing this decision-making process remain poorly understood. In this paper, we investigate its working mechanism through an information flow lens. Our findings reveal that instruction tokens function as structural anchors for modality arbitration: Shallow attention layers perform non-selective information transfer, routing multimodal cues to these anchors as a latent buffer; Modality competition is resolved within deep attention layers guided by the instruction intent, while MLP layers exhibit semantic inertia, acting as an adversarial force. Furthermore, we identify a sparse set of specialized attention heads that drive this arbitration. Causal interventions demonstrate that manipulating a mere of these critical heads can decrease the modality-following ratio by through blocking, or increase it by through targeted amplification of failed samples. Our work provides a substantial step toward model transparency and offers a principled framework for the orchestration of multimodal information in MLLMs.

Key Techniques

  • Instruction token anchoring
  • modality competition resolution in deep layers
  • sparse specialized attention head manipulation

Modality Competition Resolution (MCR)

Core Idea: A layered mechanism where deep attention layers resolve conflicts between competing modalities by applying instruction-intent-guided attention weights and gating.

Problem It Solves: When multiple modalities contain contradictory or conflicting information, MLLMs must choose which to prioritize—this decision must respect user instructions rather than arbitrary modality dominance.

How It Works:

  1. Multimodal information is buffered at instruction anchor positions in shallow layers
  2. Deep attention layers examine instruction tokens alongside multimodal cues to extract instruction intent (semantic understanding of what the user asked for)
  3. Instruction intent is used to compute modality-specific attention weights: where is each modality
  4. MLP layers apply these weights but exhibit “semantic inertia”—a bias toward preserving input semantic patterns that can work against explicit instructions

Where It’s Useful:

  • Vision-language model design with instruction-following guarantees
  • Multi-sensory embodied AI systems requiring instruction-based mode selection
  • Safety-critical applications where incorrect modality reliance causes failures

Limitations / Caveats:

  • MLP layers act as an adversarial force, reducing modality-following fidelity; addressing this requires either MLP redesign or stronger instruction signals
  • The mechanism may struggle with ambiguous or contradictory instructions
  • Effectiveness depends on instruction clarity in the input

Internal Use Cases:

  • Complements Collaborative Instruction Tuning by grounding instruction following in mechanistic layer-wise understanding
  • Could inform ranking systems where instructions conflict with collaborative signals

Causal Intervention on Critical Attention Heads (CIAH)

Core Idea: Identification and targeted manipulation of a sparse set of critical attention heads (≈5% of total) to dramatically shift modality-following behavior through blocking or amplification.

Problem It Solves: Understanding which model components actually drive modality arbitration decisions; enables precise, surgical interventions without full model retraining.

How It Works:

  1. Identify all attention heads across all layers:
  2. Define causal intervention : either blocking (zeroing attention outputs) or amplification (scaling gradients on failed samples)
  3. Apply intervention to subsets of heads and measure impact on modality-following ratio (MFR):
  4. Identify critical heads as those where exceeds threshold when intervened (paper shows ±60% shifts possible)
  5. Blocking: Set to disable modality routing
  6. Amplification: Increase gradient signal to critical heads during training on samples where modality-following failed

Where It’s Useful:

  • Model steering and behavioral control without retraining entire models
  • Safety intervention in deployed MLLMs
  • Understanding attention mechanisms in other transformer-based multimodal systems
  • Interpretability research on which components drive specific behaviors

Limitations / Caveats:

  • Interventions are task/dataset-specific; critical heads may differ across modality combinations
  • Blocking all critical heads may cause catastrophic failure in other capabilities (side effects not discussed in abstract)
  • Requires clear definition and measurement of modality-following ratio for the target domain
  • Sparse set size (5%) suggests high variance—not all heads contribute equally

Internal Use Cases:

  • Could apply to ranking systems where specific “arbitration heads” choose between collaborative signals and content features
  • Relates to Dynamic Context Assembly by potentially controlling which information components get routed through attention pathways

Semantic Inertia in MLP Layers (SI)

Core Idea: MLP layers exhibit a resistance to changing semantic patterns from their inputs, acting as an adversarial force that opposes instruction-driven modality arbitration decisions.

Problem It Solves: MLPs preserve input semantic structure through their projections, which can override or contradict explicit instruction intent, reducing overall modality-following fidelity.

How It Works:

  1. Instruction-guided attention layers compute target modality weights and route information accordingly
  2. MLP layer processes these routed representations through:
  3. The learned weights encode statistical patterns from training data that favor certain modality combinations
  4. This causes semantic “stickiness”—the MLP output preserves the original modality dominance patterns despite attention-layer routing decisions
  5. Result: instruction-following is weakened because MLPs revert to learned priors

Where It’s Useful:

  • MLP redesign for instruction-aligned systems
  • Understanding why adapter-based instruction-tuning sometimes fails
  • Improving modality arbitration robustness in multimodal fine-tuning

Limitations / Caveats:

  • The exact mechanism of semantic inertia (dropout in training? weight initialization? learned priors?) is not fully characterized
  • May not apply uniformly to all MLP layers or all types of semantic patterns
  • Fixing this may require architectural changes (e.g., modality-specific MLP branches, gating) which add complexity

Internal Use Cases:

  • Similar to issues in recommendation systems where collaborative filtering MLPs override content-based instructions
  • Could inform Collaborative Instruction Tuning by addressing how to make MLPs honor instruction signals rather than inertial collaborative patterns
