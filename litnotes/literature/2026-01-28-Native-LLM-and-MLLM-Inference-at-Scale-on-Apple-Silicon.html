Native LLM and MLLM Inference at Scale on Apple Silicon

Source: arXiv:cs.LG Published: 2026-01-27 Rank: #12 (score: 0.96, max_sim: 0.85) Reading time: ~20 min Tags: RAG, LLM, RecSys, NSA, Speculative-Decoding

Abstract

The growing adoption of Apple Silicon for machine learning development has created demand for efficient inference solutions that leverage its unique unified memory architecture. However, existing tools either lack native optimization (PyTorch MPS) or focus solely on text models (llama.cpp), leaving multimodal workloads underserved. We present vllm-mlx, a framework for efficient LLM and MLLM inference on Apple Silicon built natively on MLX. For text models, we achieve 21% to 87% higher throughput than llama.cpp across models ranging from Qwen3-0.6B to Nemotron-30B, while providing continuous batching that scales to 4.3x aggregate throughput at 16 concurrent requests. For multimodal models, we introduce content-based prefix caching that eliminates redundant vision encoding by identifying identical images through content hashing, regardless of input format. Our evaluation on Apple M4 Max demonstrates throughput of up to 525 tokens per second on text models and 28x speedup on repeated image queries, reducing multimodal latency from 21.7 seconds to under 1 second. Video analysis with up to 64 frames achieves 24.7x cache speedup. We release our implementation as open source to support efficient inference on consumer Apple hardware.

Key Techniques

  • Continuous batching
  • content-based prefix caching

Analysis: vllm-mlx and the Team Mental Model

The vllm-mlx paper fundamentally validates the core invariant from your “LLM Inference Performance” note: that per-token cost breaks down into weight compute, attention-on-context, and KV-cache reads. However, it shifts the optimization focus from general-purpose hardware to Apple Silicon’s unified memory architecture, which compresses the traditional gap between these terms. By building natively on MLX, vllm-mlx demonstrates that the team mental model’s framework—distinguishing between prefill (throughput-optimized) and decode (bandwidth-bound) phases—remains valid even on consumer hardware. The continuous batching achieving 4.3x aggregate throughput under 16 concurrent requests shows the paper is optimizing TPOT (time per output token) through better utilization of the decode phase, where KV-cache bandwidth typically becomes the bottleneck. The unified memory eliminates separate GPU/CPU memory transfers, effectively “shrinking the KV-cache reads term” through hardware-level optimization rather than algorithmic innovation.

The content-based prefix caching mechanism for multimodal models introduces a new dimension to KV-cache optimization that complements your “Sparse Attention Retrieval-Augmented KV Cache Integration” note. Rather than dynamically retrieving relevant context during inference, vllm-mlx’s approach identifies redundant computation statically through content hashing of images. This is less about sparse retrieval and more about eliminating wasteful recomputation—a form of prefix sharing that works orthogonally to dense attention. The 28x speedup on repeated image queries and 24.7x cache speedup on video frames suggests that for vision-heavy workloads, duplicate detection may be more impactful than sophisticated attention sparsity patterns. This extends the knowledge base by showing that when context redundancy is high (multiple frames of the same video, batch processing identical images), simple content-based deduplication can outperform learned sparse attention schemes.

What the paper adds to your notes is the recognition that inference efficiency on constrained hardware requires co-design between algorithm and architecture. The 21–87% throughput gains over llama.cpp aren’t primarily from novel attention patterns or sparsity—they come from aligned matrix operation kernels and memory hierarchy exploitation specific to Apple Silicon. This suggests the team mental model’s terms (active-weight compute, attention, KV-bandwidth) need to be re-weighted depending on the target device. On unified-memory Apple Silicon, making “hardware run closer to peak” (your phrase) may matter more than algorithmic tricks, flipping optimization priorities compared to datacenter GPUs where memory is the primary constraint.
