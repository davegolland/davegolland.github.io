Balancing Faithfulness and Performance in Reasoning via Multi-Listener Soft Execution

Source: arXiv:cs.CL Published: 2026-02-18 Rank: #3 (score: 0.98, max_sim: 0.83) Reading time: ~20 min Tags: RL, LLM

Abstract

Chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning sometimes fails to faithfully reflect the true computation of a large language model (LLM), hampering its utility in explaining how LLMs arrive at their answers. Moreover, optimizing for faithfulness and interpretability in reasoning often degrades task performance. To address this tradeoff and improve CoT faithfulness, we propose Reasoning Execution by Multiple Listeners (REMUL), a multi-party reinforcement learning approach. REMUL builds on the hypothesis that reasoning traces which other parties can follow will be more faithful. A speaker model generates a reasoning trace, which is truncated and passed to a pool of listener models who “execute” the trace, continuing the trace to an answer. Speakers are rewarded for producing reasoning that is clear to listeners, with additional correctness regularization via masked supervised finetuning to counter the tradeoff between faithfulness and performance. On multiple reasoning benchmarks (BIG-Bench Extra Hard, MuSR, ZebraLogicBench, and FOLIO), REMUL consistently and substantially improves three measures of faithfulness — hint attribution, early answering area over the curve (AOC), and mistake injection AOC — while also improving accuracy. Our analysis finds that these gains are robust across training domains, translate to legibility gains, and are associated with shorter and more direct CoTs.

Reasoning Execution by Multiple Listeners (REMUL)

Core Idea: A multi-party reinforcement learning framework where a speaker LLM is trained to produce chain-of-thought traces that listener LLMs can independently follow and execute to the correct answer, incentivizing faithful reasoning through communicative pressure.

Problem It Solves: Chain-of-thought reasoning is often unfaithful — the visible reasoning trace does not reflect the model’s true computation — and prior approaches to improving faithfulness typically degrade task accuracy, creating a tradeoff that REMUL breaks by using communicative legibility as a proxy for faithfulness.

How It Works:

  1. Speaker generates trace: A speaker model produces a reasoning trace for a given problem, which is truncated at an intermediate point.
  2. Listeners execute trace: A pool of listener models receives the truncated trace (without the original problem) and independently continues it to a final answer.
  3. Reward signal: The speaker is rewarded when listeners arrive at the correct answer, incentivizing traces that are self-contained and executable by others.
  4. Correctness regularization: Masked supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on correct completions is applied to the speaker to counteract any faithfulness-vs-accuracy tradeoff introduced by listener optimization alone.

Architecture:

flowchart LR
    P[Problem] --> S[Speaker Model]
    S -->|Full reasoning trace| T[Truncate]
    T -->|Partial trace| L1[Listener 1]
    T -->|Partial trace| L2[Listener 2]
    T -->|Partial trace| LN[Listener N]
    L1 & L2 & LN -->|Answers| R[Reward: correct answers]
    R -->|RL gradient| S
    GT[Ground Truth] -->|Masked SFT| S

Where It’s Useful:

  • Reasoning tasks requiring interpretable, auditable chain-of-thought (logic puzzles, math, multi-step inference)
  • Settings where faithfulness matters for trust or debugging (e.g., medical reasoning, legal analysis)
  • Benchmarks: BIG-Bench Extra Hard, MuSR, ZebraLogicBench, FOLIO

Limitations / Caveats:

  • Requires maintaining a pool of listener models during training, increasing compute cost relative to standard RL fine-tuning.
  • Faithfulness gains are measured via proxy metrics (hint attribution, early answering AOC, mistake injection AOC) — not direct mechanistic verification.
  • Speaker and listener models may converge on a shared “dialect” that is legible to each other but not to humans, limiting true interpretability gains.

Internal Use Cases:

  • LLM reasoning pipelines: Apply REMUL-style training to reasoning models used in complex query understanding or multi-hop retrieval, improving trace legibility for debugging.
  • Ranking/RecSys explanation: If using LLMs to generate explanations for recommendations, REMUL’s communicative pressure could produce explanations that are more faithfully grounded in the model’s actual scoring logic.
  • Agent evaluation: Use the listener-execution paradigm as an offline faithfulness probe — truncate agent traces and measure how well a separate model can reproduce the agent’s conclusions.
