Self-Distillation Enables Continual Learning

Source: arXiv:cs.LG Published: 2026-01-27 Rank: #13 (score: 0.91, max_sim: 0.82) Reading time: ~15 min Tags: RAG, RecSys, RL

Abstract

Continual learning, enabling models to acquire new skills and knowledge without degrading existing capabilities, remains a fundamental challenge for foundation models. While on-policy reinforcement learning can reduce forgetting, it requires explicit reward functions that are often unavailable. Learning from expert demonstrations, the primary alternative, is dominated by supervised fine-tuning (SFT), which is inherently off-policy. We introduce Self-Distillation Fine-Tuning (SDFT), a simple method that enables on-policy learning directly from demonstrations. SDFT leverages in-context learning by using a demonstration-conditioned model as its own teacher, generating on-policy training signals that preserve prior capabilities while acquiring new skills. Across skill learning and knowledge acquisition tasks, SDFT consistently outperforms SFT, achieving higher new-task accuracy while substantially reducing catastrophic forgetting. In sequential learning experiments, SDFT enables a single model to accumulate multiple skills over time without performance regression, establishing on-policy distillation as a practical path to continual learning from demonstrations.

Extending Knowledge Distillation to the On-Policy Learning Problem

This paper fundamentally reframes knowledge distillation’s purpose within continual learning. While traditional knowledge distillation (as discussed in the matched notes) focuses on compressing large teacher models into smaller students or generating synthetic training data, SDFT inverts this paradigm by having a single model serve as both teacher and student in an on-policy setting. The key innovation is using a demonstration-conditioned model to generate its own training signals, which directly addresses the resource efficiency concerns highlighted in “Continual Distillation for Lightweight Adaptation.” However, SDFT goes further by solving a distinct problem: the off-policy nature of supervised fine-tuning causes catastrophic forgetting. By maintaining on-policy learning dynamics through self-distillation, the paper creates a path for continual skill accumulation that the matched notes don’t explicitly address. This is particularly relevant to resource-constrained settings, as generating on-policy training signals without explicit reward functions eliminates the need for large replay buffers or complex RL infrastructure.

Bridging In-Context Learning with Continual Adaptation

The paper’s use of “demonstration-conditioned” models to enable on-policy learning connects directly to in-context learning capabilities now understood through the lens of foundation models. While “1000 Layer Networks for Self-Supervised Reinforcement Learning” explores how network depth enables unsupervised goal-conditioned behavior, SDFT demonstrates that foundation models’ in-context learning ability—their capacity to condition on demonstrations—creates a natural mechanism for on-policy learning without explicit reward signals. This bridges two typically separate domains: the self-supervised RL work that relies on exploration in reward-free settings, and the supervised learning paradigm that dominates foundation model adaptation. SDFT essentially shows that in-context learning is itself a form of on-policy signal generation when properly structured through distillation.

New Contribution: Practical Continual Learning Without Reward Engineering

The paper’s primary novelty lies in demonstrating that continual learning from demonstrations is achievable without either (1) expensive on-policy RL with hand-crafted rewards, or (2) the forgetting inherent to off-policy SFT. By showing that sequential skill accumulation is possible through demonstration-conditioned self-distillation, SDFT opens a practical avenue for foundation models to expand capabilities over time. This directly complements the knowledge distillation framework discussed in the matched notes by showing how distillation can be used not just for compression or data generation, but as a mechanism to preserve and integrate previously learned capabilities while acquiring new ones—a continual learning guarantee that traditional distillation approaches don’t explicitly provide.
