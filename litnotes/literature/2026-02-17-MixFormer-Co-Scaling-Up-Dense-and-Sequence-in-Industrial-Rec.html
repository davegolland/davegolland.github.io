MixFormer: Co-Scaling Up Dense and Sequence in Industrial Recommenders

Source: arXiv:cs.IR Published: 2026-02-15 Rank: #10 (score: 0.96, max_sim: 0.84) Reading time: ~20 min Tags: LLM, RAG, Transformer, RecSys

Abstract

As industrial recommender systems enter a scaling-driven regime, Transformer architectures have become increasingly attractive for scaling models towards larger capacity and longer sequence. However, existing Transformer-based recommendation models remain structurally fragmented, where sequence modeling and feature interaction are implemented as separate modules with independent parameterization. Such designs introduce a fundamental co-scaling challenge, as model capacity must be suboptimally allocated between dense feature interaction and sequence modeling under a limited computational budget. In this work, we propose MixFormer, a unified Transformer-style architecture tailored for recommender systems, which jointly models sequential behaviors and feature interactions within a single backbone. Through a unified parameterization, MixFormer enables effective co-scaling across both dense capacity and sequence length, mitigating the trade-off observed in decoupled designs. Moreover, the integrated architecture facilitates deep interaction between sequential and non-sequential representations, allowing high-order feature semantics to directly inform sequence aggregation and enhancing overall expressiveness. To ensure industrial practicality, we further introduce a user-item decoupling strategy for efficiency optimizations that significantly reduce redundant computation and inference latency. Extensive experiments on large-scale industrial datasets demonstrate that MixFormer consistently exhibits superior accuracy and efficiency. Furthermore, large-scale online A/B tests on two production recommender systems, Douyin and Douyin Lite, show consistent improvements in user engagement metrics, including active days and in-app usage duration.

MixFormer

Core Idea: A unified Transformer backbone that jointly parameterizes dense feature interaction and sequential behavior modeling in a single architecture, enabling co-scaling of both dimensions under a shared computational budget.

Problem It Solves: Existing Transformer-based recommenders decouple sequence modeling and feature interaction into separate modules with independent parameters, forcing suboptimal capacity allocation between the two when scaling under a fixed compute budget — you can’t scale both simultaneously without one cannibalizing the other.

How It Works:

  1. Unified backbone: Rather than a separate MLP tower for feature interactions and a separate Transformer for sequence modeling, MixFormer processes both through shared Transformer layers — dense feature embeddings and sequential item tokens are co-encoded in a single forward pass.
  2. Joint parameterization: Attention mechanisms simultaneously attend over non-sequential (dense) feature representations and sequential behavior tokens, allowing high-order feature semantics to directly gate and weight sequence aggregation (i.e., item importance in the history is informed by user/context features).
  3. User-item decoupling for inference: At serving time, item-side representations are precomputed and cached independently of user-side computation, eliminating redundant re-encoding of items across user requests and reducing inference latency to practical industrial levels.

Architecture:

flowchart LR
    A[Dense Features\nuser/context/item attrs] --> C[Unified MixFormer\nTransformer Layers]
    B[Sequential Tokens\nuser history events] --> C
    C --> D[Joint Attention\ndense ↔ sequence]
    D --> E[Ranking Score]
    D --> F[User-side cache]
    D --> G[Item-side cache\nprecomputed]
    F & G --> E

Where It’s Useful:

  • Industrial-scale recommender systems where user history length and feature interaction depth both need to grow
  • Ranking models on platforms with rich behavioral logs (watch history, clicks, dwell time) alongside dense categorical/continuous features
  • Any retrieval or ranking pipeline where compute budget forces a trade-off between model capacity and context length

Limitations / Caveats:

  • Unified architecture increases training complexity — joint optimization of heterogeneous inputs (dense + sequential) requires careful initialization and potentially curriculum strategies
  • User-item decoupling assumes item representations can be usefully separated from user context at inference; this approximation may lose some cross-attention fidelity
  • Industrial results are on Douyin-scale data; benefits at smaller data regimes are unverified

Internal Use Cases:

  • Co-scaling experiments: MixFormer directly operationalizes the insight from Context-Length Scaling in Sequential Recommender Transformers — instead of choosing between longer sequences or bigger dense towers, MixFormer scales both jointly; worth testing whether the same scaling curves hold under unified vs. decoupled parameterization
  • HSTU comparison: Hierarchical Sequential Transduction Unit (HSTU) handles long histories via hierarchical simplifications for efficiency; MixFormer takes the opposite bet (full Transformer, but unified) — the user-item decoupling trick is the analogous efficiency lever; interesting ablation target
  • Parameter efficiency angle: TokenFormer’s tokenized-parameter framing suggests MixFormer’s dense features could themselves be treated as “parameter tokens,” potentially enabling incremental capacity scaling without full retraining
