Reliability by Design: Quantifying and Eliminating Fabrication Risk in LLMs

Reliability by Design: Quantifying and Eliminating Fabrication Risk in LLMs

Summary

This paper tackles a fundamental question: how do we make LLMs trustworthy for high-stakes work? The authors introduce two new metrics for measuring fabrication, evaluate 12 LLMs across 75 legal tasks with expert review, and show that advanced RAG architectures can reduce fabrication to negligible levels (<0.2%).

The core insight is that “consultative AI” (retrieval-based, verification-focused) is fundamentally different from “generative AI” (creative, unconstrained)—and high-stakes domains need the former.

The Two Metrics

False Citation Rate (FCR)

Percentage of citations that are:

  • Nonexistent (made-up case law, fake papers)
  • Misattributed (real source, wrong claim)
  • Irrelevant (real source, doesn’t support the point)

Standalone LLMs show >30% FCR—nearly 1 in 3 citations is wrong. This is disqualifying for legal work.

Fabricated Fact Rate (FFR)

Percentage of factual assertions that are invented rather than grounded in source material. Measures the “confident bullshit” problem where models state things authoritatively that have no basis.

These metrics separate two failure modes: bad sourcing (FCR) vs bad claims (FFR). A system could cite real sources but make wrong claims, or vice versa.

The Three Paradigms

The paper frames three approaches as characters:

1. “Creative Oracle” (Standalone LLM)

No retrieval, pure generation. FCR >30%, unsuitable for professional use. The model just makes things up when it doesn’t know—and it doesn’t know it doesn’t know.

2. “Expert Archivist” (Basic RAG)

Retrieves relevant documents, generates grounded responses. Dramatically better, but still has 10-15% error rates. The problem: retrieval errors propagate to generation, and the model still fills gaps creatively.

3. “Rigorous Archivist” (Advanced RAG)

The full stack: embedding fine-tuning + re-ranking + self-correction loops. Achieves <0.2% fabrication.

The Self-Correction Loop

This is the key technique beyond standard RAG:

  1. Generate initial response with citations
  2. Verify each citation — Does it actually exist? Does it support the claim?
  3. Check for gaps — Are there unsourced claims?
  4. Regenerate with corrections

This is expensive (multiple LLM calls per response) but necessary for high-stakes use. The system essentially reviews its own work before submitting.

The verification step is critical: the model checks “did I actually get this from the retrieved documents, or did I make it up?” This catches the subtle hallucinations that slip through basic RAG.

Why 30% FCR is So Bad

Consider: if 1 in 3 citations is wrong, and a legal brief has 30 citations, you’d expect ~10 fabricated references. Any opposing counsel who checks will destroy your credibility. This is why the paper concludes standalone LLMs are “unsuitable for professional use” in legal contexts—not just suboptimal, but dangerous.

Connections

  • RAG — Complements the chunking paper; both show advanced RAG >> basic RAG
  • hallucination — FCR and FFR are concrete, measurable definitions of hallucination
  • autonomous research — For any system generating literature notes with citations, FCR is the metric to track
  • self-improving agents — The self-correction loop is a form of self-critique, related to constitutional AI and self-refinement patterns

Practical Takeaways

  1. Measure fabrication explicitly — FCR and FFR should be standard metrics for any RAG system
  2. Self-correction is expensive but necessary for high-stakes domains
  3. Embedding fine-tuning helps — Domain-specific embeddings catch more relevant chunks
  4. The gap between “impressive demo” and “professional-grade” is 30x (30% → <1% error)

Application to Our Work

For the autonomous research system, we should:

  1. Verify every [wikilink](/refs/wikilink/) actually exists in the vault before approving
  2. Track approval/rejection rates as a proxy for synthesis quality
  3. Consider a self-correction pass that checks “does this claim appear in the source material?”

Questions for Review

  • Should we implement FCR tracking for synthesized literature notes?
  • What would a self-correction loop look like in our pipeline?
  • Is 0.2% fabrication rate achievable with current models + good RAG?
