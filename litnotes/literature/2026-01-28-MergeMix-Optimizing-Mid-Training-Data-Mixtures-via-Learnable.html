MergeMix: Optimizing Mid-Training Data Mixtures via Learnable Model Merging

Source: arXiv:cs.AI Published: 2026-01-25 Rank: #9 (score: 0.99, max_sim: 0.83) Reading time: ~15 min Tags: RAG, Search, LLM

Abstract

Optimizing data mixtures is essential for unlocking the full potential of large language models (LLMs), yet identifying the optimal composition remains computationally prohibitive due to reliance on heuristic trials or expensive proxy training. To address this, we introduce \textbf{MergeMix}, a novel approach that efficiently determines optimal data mixing ratios by repurposing model merging weights as a high-fidelity, low-cost performance proxy. By training domain-specific experts on minimal tokens and optimizing their merging weights against downstream benchmarks, MergeMix effectively optimizes the performance of data mixtures without incurring the cost of full-scale training. Extensive experiments on models with 8B and 16B parameters validate that MergeMix achieves performance comparable to or surpassing exhaustive manual tuning while drastically reducing search costs. Furthermore, MergeMix exhibits high rank consistency (Spearman ) and strong cross-scale transferability, offering a scalable, automated solution for data mixture optimization.

Key Techniques

Analysis: MergeMix in Context of Prior Work

Connection to Data Efficiency and Model Scaling: MergeMix directly extends the efficiency-focused agenda established in the vault notes, particularly “Data-Efficient Dense Retrieval via LLM Scale.” While that work achieves efficiency by initializing retrieval encoders with large-scale LLM backbones and leveraging pre-training, MergeMix tackles a complementary problem: how to efficiently optimize which data to train on. Both papers share the core insight that intelligent shortcuts—whether through strategic initialization or smart data selection—can dramatically reduce computational overhead compared to exhaustive trial-and-error. However, MergeMix introduces a novel intermediary: instead of searching the data mixture space directly, it uses model merging weights as a lightweight proxy for performance prediction, effectively turning an expensive discrete search problem into a continuous optimization problem with high fidelity.

Novel Contribution Through Model Merging as a Proxy: The key innovation MergeMix brings is methodological rather than conceptual. While Drama and other notes emphasize data augmentation and model pruning to achieve efficiency, MergeMix proposes a fundamentally different approach: train multiple domain-specific expert models on minimal tokens, then optimize their merging weights to predict downstream performance without full retraining. This bridges traditional multi-expert learning (implicit in the Drama framework’s diverse augmentation) with recent advances in model merging. The Spearman ρ > 0.9 rank consistency metric suggests that the merging weight optimization landscape is nearly isomorphic to the downstream task performance landscape—a finding that challenges the common assumption that data mixture optimization requires full-scale training validation.

Expanding the Toolkit for Automated LLM Optimization: MergeMix adds a critical new perspective to the knowledge base: performance proxies matter more than traditional training. While Drama focuses on how to create diverse training signals and Llama4Rec explores mutual augmentation between specialized and general models, MergeMix identifies that you don’t need to actually train models to convergence to understand which data mixtures work best. The cross-scale transferability finding (that mixture ratios optimized on 8B models transfer to 16B models) suggests data mixture optimization might be one of the few scaling properties that doesn’t require retuning, offering practical advantages for industrial LLM development where models are constantly scaled.
