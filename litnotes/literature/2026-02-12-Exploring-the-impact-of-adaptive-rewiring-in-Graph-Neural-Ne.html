Exploring the impact of adaptive rewiring in Graph Neural Networks

Source: arXiv:cs.AI Published: 2026-02-11 Rank: #4 (score: 0.99, max_sim: 0.82) Reading time: ~20 min Tags: Search, RAG, Graph

Abstract

This paper explores sparsification methods as a form of regularization in Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) to address high memory usage and computational costs in large-scale graph applications. Using techniques from Network Science and Machine Learning, including Erdős-Rényi for model sparsification, we enhance the efficiency of GNNs for real-world applications. We demonstrate our approach on N-1 contingency assessment in electrical grids, a critical task for ensuring grid reliability. We apply our methods to three datasets of varying sizes, exploring Graph Convolutional Networks (GCN) and Graph Isomorphism Networks (GIN) with different degrees of sparsification and rewiring. Comparison across sparsification levels shows the potential of combining insights from both research fields to improve GNN performance and scalability. Our experiments highlight the importance of tuning sparsity parameters: while sparsity can improve generalization, excessive sparsity may hinder learning of complex patterns. Our adaptive rewiring approach, particularly when combined with early stopping, proves promising by allowing the model to adapt its connectivity structure during training. This research contributes to understanding how sparsity can be effectively leveraged in GNNs for critical applications like power grid reliability analysis.

Adaptive Rewiring with Erdős-Rényi Sparsification

Core Idea: Apply probabilistic edge pruning based on Erdős-Rényi random graph theory during GNN training, allowing the model to dynamically adjust its connectivity structure to balance memory efficiency with learning capacity for critical pattern recognition tasks.

Problem It Solves: GNNs on large-scale graphs (e.g., electrical grids) face prohibitive memory and computational costs; naive sparsification can destroy critical structural patterns needed for tasks like N-1 contingency assessment, while dense graphs are infeasible at scale.

How It Works:

  1. Initial Sparsification: Apply Erdős-Rényi probabilistic edge sampling to the input graph, creating a sparse adjacency matrix with controlled edge probability (sparsity parameter)
  2. Adaptive Training: During GNN training (GCN or GIN), allow the model to rewire connections—adding or removing edges based on gradient signals and learned importance
  3. Early Stopping Integration: Combine adaptive rewiring with early stopping to prevent over-sparsification; halt training when validation performance plateaus to preserve learned connectivity patterns
  4. Sparsity-Accuracy Trade-off Tuning: Systematically explore sparsity levels to find the optimal balance where regularization improves generalization without preventing learning of complex structural patterns

Where It’s Useful:

  • Critical infrastructure reliability analysis (power grid N-1 contingency assessment)
  • Large-scale graph applications where memory constraints limit model depth/width
  • Domains requiring interpretable graph structure after training (rewired edges reveal learned importance)

Limitations / Caveats:

  • Excessive sparsity can hinder learning of complex patterns—requires careful tuning of edge probability
  • Erdős-Rényi random sampling may discard structurally critical edges (e.g., bridges in power grids); domain-aware sampling could improve results
  • Adaptive rewiring adds hyperparameters (rewiring rate, rewiring strategy) that require validation-based tuning
  • Performance gains are task-dependent; works best when input graphs have redundant edges

Internal Use Cases:

  • RecSys graph pruning: Apply to user-item or social graphs to reduce memory in GNN-based collaborative filtering while maintaining recommendation quality through adaptive rewiring
  • Knowledge graph completion: Sparsify dense KG embeddings during pre-training, allowing model to rewire and focus on high-signal relations for downstream retrieval/ranking
  • LLM retrieval graphs: Build sparse document similarity graphs for retrieval-augmented generation, using adaptive rewiring to discover non-obvious semantic connections during fine-tuning
