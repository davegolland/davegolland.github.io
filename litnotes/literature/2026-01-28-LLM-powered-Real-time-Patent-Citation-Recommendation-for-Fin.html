LLM-powered Real-time Patent Citation Recommendation for Financial Technologies

Source: arXiv:cs.IR Published: 2026-01-23 Rank: #9 (score: 1.19, max_sim: 0.76) Reading time: ~20 min Tags: LLM, RecSys, RAG, Embeddings, Search

Abstract

Rapid financial innovation has been accompanied by a sharp increase in patenting activity, making timely and comprehensive prior-art discovery more difficult. This problem is especially evident in financial technologies, where innovations develop quickly, patent collections grow continuously, and citation recommendation systems must be updated as new applications arrive. Existing patent retrieval and citation recommendation methods typically rely on static indexes or periodic retraining, which limits their ability to operate effectively in such dynamic settings. In this study, we propose a real-time patent citation recommendation framework designed for large and fast-changing financial patent corpora. Using a dataset of 428,843 financial patents granted by the China National Intellectual Property Administration (CNIPA) between 2000 and 2024, we build a three-stage recommendation pipeline. The pipeline uses large language model (LLM) embeddings to represent the semantic content of patent abstracts, applies efficient approximate nearest-neighbor search to construct a manageable candidate set, and ranks candidates by semantic similarity to produce top-k citation recommendations. In addition to improving recommendation accuracy, the proposed framework directly addresses the dynamic nature of patent systems. By using an incremental indexing strategy based on hierarchical navigable small-world (HNSW) graphs, newly issued patents can be added without rebuilding the entire index. A rolling day-by-day update experiment shows that incremental updating improves recall while substantially reducing computational cost compared with rebuild-based indexing. The proposed method also consistently outperforms traditional text-based baselines and alternative nearest-neighbor retrieval approaches.

Analysis: Patent Citation Recommendation Through the Lens of Retrieval Evolution

This patent citation recommendation framework represents a practical instantiation of the retrieval and ranking evolution outlined in Traditional recommender system techniques—moving from deterministic matching toward semantic retrieval with efficient indexing. The paper’s three-stage pipeline (LLM embeddings → approximate nearest-neighbor search → semantic ranking) directly mirrors the progression from simple content-based methods to hybrid systems that incorporate side information and latent representations. Where traditional techniques advocate “start simple, add latent generalization, then add side-features,” this work concretely demonstrates that progression: treating patent abstracts as semantic features via LLM embeddings, using HNSW graphs as an efficient indexing mechanism (solving the “indexing and latency constraints” problem at scale), and ranking by similarity scores. Critically, the incremental indexing strategy addresses the “freshness” challenge identified in the traditional techniques note—new patents arrive continuously in patent systems just as new items flow into real-time recommendation systems, and rebuilding indexes periodically becomes prohibitively expensive.

The connection to Generative Large Recommendation Model: Emerging Trends in LLM for Recommendation is more subtle but consequential. While that survey focuses on LLM-enhanced reasoning and generation for recommendation, this patent work uses LLMs primarily as embedding generators—leveraging their semantic understanding to represent patent content rather than using them for end-to-end generation or ranking. This represents a pragmatic middle ground: embedding-based retrieval scales to 428K+ patents efficiently, while full LLM ranking of all candidates would be computationally prohibitive. The paper thus demonstrates a constraint-aware integration of LLM capabilities—using them where their strengths (semantic understanding) matter most without requiring them in the latency-critical path.

Finally, the framework extends LLM-Generated Synthetic Queries for Long-Tail Retrieval in inverse form. Rather than generating synthetic queries to improve retrieval of rare items, this work uses LLM embeddings to improve citation discovery for patent innovations—which are inherently long-tail phenomena in large patent corpora. New financial technology patents are sparse, semantically novel, and historically difficult to match with prior art; embedding-based retrieval naturally handles this better than keyword matching or traditional CF approaches. The incremental update mechanism also solves the catalog-expansion problem implicit in that note: as the patent corpus grows continuously, the retrieval system adapts without retraining, maintaining coverage of long-tail innovations even as the distribution shifts.
