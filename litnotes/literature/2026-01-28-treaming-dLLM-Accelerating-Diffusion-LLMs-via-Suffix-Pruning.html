treaming-dLLM: Accelerating Diffusion LLMs via Suffix Pruning and Dynamic Decoding

Source: arXiv:cs.CL Published: 2026-01-25 Rank: #6 (score: 0.99, max_sim: 0.83) Reading time: ~20 min Tags: Diffusion, RAG, LLM Code: https://github.com/xiaoshideta/Streaming-dLLM

Abstract

Diffusion Large Language Models (dLLMs) offer a compelling paradigm for natural language generation, leveraging parallel decoding and bidirectional attention to achieve superior global coherence compared to autoregressive models. While recent works have accelerated inference via KV cache reuse or heuristic decoding, they overlook the intrinsic inefficiencies within the block-wise diffusion process. Specifically, they suffer from spatial redundancy by modeling informative-sparse suffix regions uniformly and temporal inefficiency by applying fixed denoising schedules across all the decoding process. To address this, we propose Streaming-dLLM, a training-free framework that streamlines inference across both spatial and temporal dimensions. Spatially, we introduce attenuation guided suffix modeling to approximate the full context by pruning redundant mask tokens. Temporally, we employ a dynamic confidence aware strategy with an early exit mechanism, allowing the model to skip unnecessary iterations for converged tokens. Extensive experiments show that Streaming-dLLM achieves up to 68.2X speedup while maintaining generation quality, highlighting its effectiveness in diffusion decoding. The code is available at https://github.com/xiaoshideta/Streaming-dLLM.

Extending Efficiency Work Across Different Paradigms

The Streaming-dLLM paper extends the efficiency acceleration concerns found in the matched notes but targets a fundamentally different inference paradigm. While “Accelerating Inference of Retrieval-Augmented Generation via Sparse Context Selection” focuses on reducing computational overhead through sparse retrieval, and “Pruning LLMs for Retriever Backbones” optimizes model size through structured pruning, Streaming-dLLM addresses inefficiencies within the diffusion decoding process itself—a parallel decoding approach that contrasts with the sequential, autoregressive generation assumed in the retrieval-augmented work. This represents a parallel axis of optimization: rather than pruning parameters or selecting sparse contexts upstream, Streaming-dLLM prunes redundant computation during inference through spatial suffix reduction and temporal early-exit mechanisms. The connection is methodological rather than architectural—all three papers recognize that modern LLM systems suffer from unnecessary computational overhead and propose training-free or low-cost solutions to eliminate it.

Bridging Spatial and Temporal Redundancy Reduction

Streaming-dLLM’s core innovation—distinguishing between spatial redundancy (informative-sparse suffix regions) and temporal inefficiency (fixed denoising schedules)—offers a more granular vocabulary for understanding inefficiency than appears in the matched notes. The pruning work in “Pruning LLMs for Retriever Backbones” addresses model-level redundancy (unnecessary heads, layers, dimensions), while Streaming-dLLM reveals that even within a single forward pass, diffusion models exhibit region-specific (spatial) and iteration-specific (temporal) inefficiencies. The “attenuation guided suffix modeling” technique—approximating full context by pruning mask tokens—mirrors the principle behind sparse context selection in retrieval-augmented systems: not all parts of the input carry equal information density, and computational resources can be redistributed by recognizing this asymmetry.

Novel Perspective: Dynamic Decoding as a Confidence-Aware Strategy

Where the matched notes treat efficiency largely as a parameter or context reduction problem, Streaming-dLLM introduces a temporal dimension via confidence-aware early exit, allowing different tokens to converge at different iteration counts. This perspective is absent from the matched vault notes and represents a valuable addition: rather than applying uniform computation budgets across all tokens (as implicit in both retrieval-augmented and pruned retriever approaches), Streaming-dLLM treats decoding as an adaptive process where per-token confidence metrics guide resource allocation. The 68.2X speedup while maintaining quality suggests that diffusion LLMs may benefit from heterogeneous computational allocation in ways that complement the spatial pruning and context sparsification strategies documented in prior work.
