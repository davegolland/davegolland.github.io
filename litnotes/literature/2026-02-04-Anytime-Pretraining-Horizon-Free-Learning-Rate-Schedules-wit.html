Anytime Pretraining: Horizon-Free Learning-Rate Schedules with Weight Averaging

Source: arXiv:cs.AI Published: 2026-02-03 Rank: #2 (score: 1.00, max_sim: 0.82) Reading time: ~20 min Tags: Embeddings, RAG, LLM, Early-Exit Code: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2602.03702v1

Abstract

Large language models are increasingly trained in continual or open-ended settings, where the total training horizon is not known in advance. Despite this, most existing pretraining recipes are not anytime: they rely on horizon-dependent learning rate schedules and extensive tuning under a fixed compute budget. In this work, we provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating the existence of anytime learning schedules for overparameterized linear regression, and we highlight the central role of weight averaging - also known as model merging - in achieving the minimax convergence rates of stochastic gradient descent. We show that these anytime schedules polynomially decay with time, with the decay rate determined by the source and capacity conditions of the problem. Empirically, we evaluate 150M and 300M parameter language models trained at 1-32x Chinchilla scale, comparing constant learning rates with weight averaging and schedules with weight averaging against a well-tuned cosine schedule. Across the full training range, the anytime schedules achieve comparable final loss to cosine decay. Taken together, our results suggest that weight averaging combined with simple, horizon-free step sizes offers a practical and effective anytime alternative to cosine learning rate schedules for large language model pretraining.

Key Techniques

  • Anytime learning schedules
  • weight averaging (model merging)
  • polynomial decay learning rates

Weight Averaging for Anytime Learning

Core Idea: Periodically merge model snapshots during training—averaging weights across checkpoints—to smooth the optimization landscape and recover minimax convergence rates without horizon-dependent schedules.

Problem It Solves: Polynomially-decaying learning rates alone do not achieve optimal convergence; weight averaging stabilizes the trajectory and enables anytime schedules to match the performance of well-tuned cosine schedules.

How It Works:

  1. At regular intervals during training, save model checkpoints at timesteps (t_1, t_2, \ldots, t_k)
  2. Compute the averaged model: (\bar{w} = \frac{1}{k}\sum_{i=1}^{k} w_{t_i})
  3. Continue training from the averaged weights (or use the averaged model at evaluation)
  4. The averaging acts as an implicit regularization, centering the model in a well-conditioned region of the loss landscape
  5. This combination with polynomial decay achieves convergence rates matching horizon-aware baselines

Architecture:

flowchart LR
    A["Training Step t"] --> B["Save Checkpoint"]
    B --> C{"Averaging Interval?"}
    C -->|No| A
    C -->|Yes| D["Average Weights"]
    D --> E["Merged Model"]
    E --> F["Continue Training"]
    F --> A

Where It’s Useful:

  • LLM pretraining with anytime schedules
  • Federated learning (averaging client models)
  • Continual learning with periodic consolidation
  • Model ensembling and robustness improvement

Limitations / Caveats:

  • Requires storing multiple checkpoints, increasing memory overhead
  • Averaging frequency is a hyperparameter that must be tuned
  • May slow convergence in early training phases if checkpoints are poor quality
  • Theoretical guarantees are strongest for convex/overparameterized settings

Internal Use Cases:

  • LLM pretraining at variable scales: Can enable training recipes that don’t require fixing Chinchilla-optimal compute budgets upfront, allowing flexible scaling
  • Continual learning for ranking models: Weight averaging could stabilize embedding updates in online learning systems where training horizon is unknown

Horizon-Free Convergence Analysis for Overparameterized Linear Regression

Core Idea: Provide theoretical guarantees showing that polynomially-decaying learning rates combined with weight averaging achieve minimax convergence rates for overparameterized linear regression without dependence on the training horizon T.

Problem It Solves: Previous convergence analyses of SGD assume knowledge of T to set learning rates; this work decouples the convergence rate from T, enabling truly anytime algorithms.

How It Works:

  1. Model: overparameterized linear regression (\min_w \mathbb{E}[\ell(w; z)]) where (w \in \mathbb{R}^d), (d > n) (samples)
  2. Apply SGD with polynomial decay (\eta_t = \eta_0 \cdot t^{-\alpha})
  3. Prove that under source condition (|w^|_{\mathcal{H}K} \leq R) and capacity condition (\lambda{\min}(H) \geq \lambda), the expected excess risk satisfies: (\mathbb{E}[L(w_t) - L(w^)] = O(t^{-\beta})) where (\beta) depends only on problem structure, not T
  4. Show that weight averaging preserves (or improves) these rates
  5. Constants in convergence rate scale polynomially with problem-dependent quantities, not exponentially with dimension

Where It’s Useful:

  • Theoretical foundations for anytime learning in deep learning
  • Analysis of continual learning algorithms
  • Understanding convergence under unknown horizons

Limitations / Caveats:

  • Restricted to linear models; extension to deep networks is empirical only
  • Requires explicit source and capacity conditions that may not hold in practice
  • Gap between theory (linear regression) and practice (300M-parameter Transformers)
  • Convergence rates may be loose compared to empirical optimization behavior
