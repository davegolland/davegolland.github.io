ReNeuIR at SIGIR 2024: The Third Workshop on Reaching Efficiency in Neural Information Retrieval

Source: S2:Annual International ACM SIGIR Conference on Research and Development in Information Retrieval Published: 2024 Rank: #6 (score: 0.99, max_sim: 0.82) Reading time: ~20 min Tags: LLM, Search, RAG

Abstract

The Information Retrieval (IR) community has a rich history of empirically measuring novel retrieval methods in terms of effectiveness and efficiency. However, as the search ecosystem is developing rapidly, comparatively little attention has been paid to evaluating efficiency in recent years, which raises the question of the cost-benefit ratio between effectiveness and efficiency. In this regard, it has become difficult to compare and contrast systems in an empirically fair way. Factors including hardware configurations, software versioning, experimental settings, and measurement methods all contribute to the difficulty of meaningfully comparing search systems, especially where efficiency is a key component of the evaluation. Furthermore, efficiency is no longer limited to time and space but has found new, challenging dimensions that stretch to resource, sample, and energy efficiency and have implications for users, researchers, and the environment. Examining algorithms and models through the lens of efficiency and its trade-off with effectiveness requires revisiting and establishing new standards and principles, from defining relevant concepts, to designing measures, to creating guidelines for making sense of the significance of findings. The third iteration of ReNeuIR aims to bring the community together to debate these questions and collaboratively test and improve a benchmarking framework for efficiency derived from the discussions of the first two iterations of this workshop. We provide a first prototype of this framework by organizing a shared task track focused on comparability and reproducibility at the workshop.

ReNeuIR Efficiency Benchmarking Framework

Core Idea: A standardized framework for measuring and comparing efficiency metrics (time, space, resource, sample, and energy) alongside effectiveness in neural information retrieval systems, addressing the lack of fair comparability due to varying hardware, software, and experimental settings.

Problem It Solves: Modern IR evaluations focus heavily on effectiveness (relevance, ranking quality) while neglecting efficiency, making it impossible to fairly compare the cost-benefit ratio of different neural retrieval methods across different experimental setups and hardware configurations.

How It Works:

  1. Multi-dimensional efficiency measurement: Captures not just time/space complexity but also resource utilization, sample efficiency (data requirements), and energy consumption
  2. Standardized experimental protocol: Defines fixed hardware configurations, software versions, measurement methodologies, and reporting standards for reproducibility
  3. Shared task evaluation: Provides benchmark datasets and evaluation harness where participants submit systems that are tested under identical conditions
  4. Trade-off analysis: Explicitly measures and reports the effectiveness-efficiency Pareto frontier to quantify whether gains in relevance justify computational costs

Where It’s Useful:

  • Comparing dense retrieval models vs. sparse retrieval methods on production constraints
  • Evaluating neural reranking architectures for latency-sensitive applications
  • Assessing retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) pipelines where inference cost matters
  • Green AI initiatives measuring carbon footprint of information retrieval systems

Limitations / Caveats:

  • Standardization may not capture all real-world deployment scenarios (edge devices, distributed systems)
  • Framework adoption requires community consensus and maintained infrastructure
  • Energy measurement methodology varies significantly across hardware vendors
  • May not generalize across vastly different retrieval paradigms (e.g., symbolic vs. neural)

Internal Use Cases:

  • Benchmark embedding models for RecSys against latency budgets (e.g., <50ms p99 for real-time ranking)
  • Evaluate LLM-based reranking vs. lightweight cross-encoders on GPU memory and throughput
  • Quantify sample efficiency of contrastive learning methods for retrieval when labeled data is scarce
  • Report energy consumption for dense retrieval training to guide model selection under sustainability constraints
