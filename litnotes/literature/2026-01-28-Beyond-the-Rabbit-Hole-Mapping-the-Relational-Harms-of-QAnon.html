Beyond the Rabbit Hole: Mapping the Relational Harms of QAnon Radicalization

Source: arXiv:cs.CL Published: 2026-01-25 Rank: #7 (score: 1.25, max_sim: 0.76) Reading time: ~20 min Tags: LLM, Search, RAG

Abstract

The rise of conspiracy theories has created far-reaching societal harm in the public discourse by eroding trust and fueling polarization. Beyond this public impact lies a deeply personal toll on the friends and families of conspiracy believers, a dimension often overlooked in large-scale computational research. This study fills this gap by systematically mapping radicalization journeys and quantifying the associated emotional toll inflicted on loved ones. We use the prominent case of QAnon as a case study, analyzing 12747 narratives from the r/QAnonCasualties support community through a novel mixed-methods approach. First, we use topic modeling (BERTopic) to map the radicalization trajectories, identifying key pre-existing conditions, triggers, and post-radicalization characteristics. From this, we apply an LDA-based graphical model to uncover six recurring archetypes of QAnon adherents, which we term “radicalization personas.” Finally, using LLM-assisted emotion detection and regression modeling, we link these personas to the specific emotional toll reported by narrators. Our findings reveal that these personas are not just descriptive; they are powerful predictors of the specific emotional harms experienced by narrators. Radicalization perceived as a deliberate ideological choice is associated with narrator anger and disgust, while those marked by personal and cognitive collapse are linked to fear and sadness. This work provides the first empirical framework for understanding radicalization as a relational phenomenon, offering a vital roadmap for researchers and practitioners to navigate its interpersonal fallout.

Extending the Spiral Framework

This paper operationalizes the theoretical spiral mechanisms discussed in “Why We Spiral” by mapping how conspiracy radicalization functions as a documented feedback loop with measurable emotional consequences. While Walton’s framework identifies the general psychological architecture of spirals—core questions about identity and belonging, construal patterns, and calcification—the QAnon study empirically demonstrates these dynamics in a specific high-stakes context. The six radicalization personas effectively represent different entry points into Walton’s spiral: some individuals spiral through identity-based appeals (“Who am I?”), others through belonging (“Do I belong to a community?”), and still others through existential questions that calcify into worldviews. The paper’s finding that personas rooted in “personal and cognitive collapse” trigger fear and sadness aligns with Walton’s observation that uncertain or challenging situations activate defensive psychological patterns. Crucially, the paper extends the theory by quantifying the relational toll—showing that these individual spirals have documented emotional impacts on social networks, which Walton’s framework doesn’t explicitly address.

Detecting Engineered Narratives Through Persona Mapping

The matched note on PSYOPs and engineered reality provides a complementary lens for interpreting the radicalization personas. Chase Hughes’ FATE model (Focus, Authority, Tribe, Emotion) maps manipulative communication tactics, while this paper’s persona-based approach identifies who becomes susceptible and why. The personas marked by ideological choice-based radicalization—associated with narrator anger and disgust—may represent individuals particularly responsive to the “Focus” and “Authority” components of the FATE model, while those experiencing cognitive collapse may be more vulnerable to “Tribe” and “Emotion” appeals. The paper’s LLM-assisted emotion detection methodology offers a scalable approach to identifying PSYOP impacts that Hughes emphasizes: by mapping specific emotional outcomes to radicalization patterns, the study reveals the emotional weaponization mechanisms that PSYOPs exploit. This bridges computational narrative analysis with behavioral psychology’s understanding of manipulation tactics.

New Methodological Contribution: Persona-Based Harm Quantification

The paper’s distinctive contribution is developing “radicalization personas” as a predictive framework for relational harm—a technique not directly addressed in the matched notes. By combining BERTopic for trajectory mapping, LDA graphical models for persona identification, and LLM-assisted emotion detection with regression analysis, the study creates a novel pipeline for connecting who people become (personas) to what emotional impacts they inflict on networks. This methodology extends the comment culture analysis from the third matched note by showing how online communities like r/QAnonCasualties generate structured data about harm that can be computationally analyzed. Where the comments paper examines platform affordances and community culture, this study demonstrates how support communities create a unique research corpus—narratives of radicalization from the perspective of affected loved ones—that enables harm quantification rather than just discourse analysis.
