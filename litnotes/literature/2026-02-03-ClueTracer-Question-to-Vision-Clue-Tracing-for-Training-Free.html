ClueTracer: Question-to-Vision Clue Tracing for Training-Free Hallucination Suppression in Multimodal Reasoning

Source: arXiv:cs.AI Published: 2026-02-02 Rank: #12 (score: 0.89, max_sim: 0.81) Reading time: ~0 min Tags: Uncategorized Code: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2602.02004v1

Abstract

Large multimodal reasoning models solve challenging visual problems via explicit long-chain inference: they gather visual clues from images and decode clues into textual tokens. Yet this capability also increases hallucinations, where the model generates content that is not supported by the input image or the question. To understand this failure mode, we identify \emph{reasoning drift}: during clue gathering, the model over-focuses on question-irrelevant entities, diluting focus on task-relevant cues and gradually decoupling the reasoning trace from visual grounding. As a consequence, many inference-time localization or intervention methods developed for non-reasoning models fail to pinpoint the true clues in reasoning settings. Motivated by these insights, we introduce ClueRecall, a metric for assessing visual clue retrieval, and present ClueTracer, a training-free, parameter-free, and architecture-agnostic plugin for hallucination suppression. ClueTracer starts from the question and traces how key clues propagate along the model’s reasoning pathway (question outputs visual tokens), thereby localizing task-relevant patches while suppressing spurious attention to irrelevant regions. Remarkably, \textbf{without any additional training}, ClueTracer improves all \textbf{reasoning} architectures (including \texttt{R1-OneVision}, \texttt{Ocean-R1}, \texttt{MM-Eureka}, \emph{etc}.) by on reasoning benchmarks. When transferred to \textbf{non-reasoning} settings, it yields a gain.

ClueTracer (Question-to-Vision Clue Tracing)

Core Idea: A training-free, parameter-free plugin that suppresses hallucinations by back-tracing task-relevant visual clues from the model’s outputs through its reasoning pathway, then re-weighting or masking visual attention to amplify signal from grounded regions and suppress spurious attending.

Problem It Solves: During reasoning, multimodal models suffer from reasoning drift—over-focusing on question-irrelevant entities while gradually decoupling their inference trace from visual grounding. Standard localization/intervention methods (designed for non-reasoning models) fail to pinpoint true clues in long-chain reasoning settings because they don’t account for how attention propagates across reasoning steps.

How It Works:

  1. Reverse trace from outputs to inputs: Start from the model’s generated output tokens (which may be hallucinated) and trace backward through the reasoning pathway to identify which visual tokens contributed to those outputs
  2. Identify question-relevant clues: Use the original question to filter which visual regions are task-relevant (suppress attention to question-irrelevant entities)
  3. Forward re-weighting: Propagate clue relevance forward through the reasoning chain, amplifying attention weights to grounded visual patches and attenuating spurious attended regions
  4. Generate corrected output: Use the re-weighted attention distribution to guide token generation, reducing hallucination

Where It’s Useful:

  • Inference-time hallucination suppression in multimodal reasoning models (LVLMs, VQA systems)
  • Improving performance on reasoning benchmarks without retraining
  • Enhancing factual grounding in long-chain visual reasoning tasks
  • Can be applied post-hoc to any reasoning architecture (R1-OneVision, Ocean-R1, MM-Eureka, etc.)

Limitations / Caveats:

  • Relies on ability to inspect/access intermediate attention states and reasoning pathways (may be unavailable in some black-box APIs)
  • Tracing accuracy depends on the quality of the model’s attention maps—noisy or diffuse attention can degrade clue recovery
  • May over-suppress valid inferences in cases where question-relevant context requires attending to seemingly tangential regions
  • Computational cost of tracing and re-weighting adds inference latency

Internal Use Cases:

  • Ranking systems: Could suppress low-quality recommendations by back-tracing user intent (query) and filtering items by relevance to core user signals, not spurious clicked signals
  • LLM reasoning: Suppressing reasoning drift in recommendation chains where models justify picks by over-focusing on recent/popular items instead of true user preference clues
  • Multi-stage retrieval: Re-weighting candidate rankings post-retrieval by tracing which candidates contributed to top predictions, filtering hallucinated matches

Analysis: ClueTracer and Related Work

Bridging Visual Grounding and Hallucination Mitigation

ClueTracer addresses a critical gap that emerges at the intersection of concerns raised by the matched notes. While MultiRAG and Multimodal Knowledge Graph Construction focus on mitigating hallucination through knowledge reconciliation and structural entity linking, ClueTracer identifies a more fundamental problem in reasoning-specific multimodal models: reasoning drift—the decoupling of reasoning traces from visual grounding. This extends prior hallucination mitigation work by pinpointing that standard localization methods fail in reasoning settings because they don’t account for how task-relevant cues propagate through long inference chains. ClueTracer’s question-to-vision tracing mechanism (question → outputs → visual tokens) directly addresses what MultiRAG treats implicitly: the need to validate that intermediate reasoning steps remain grounded in source information. Where MultiRAG aggregates and reconciles multi-source information, ClueTracer backtracks through the model’s own reasoning pathway to verify alignment—a training-free approach that complements knowledge-external approaches.

Methodological Novelty in Reasoning-Aware Grounding

The paper introduces two key methodological contributions missing from the matched notes: (1) ClueRecall, a metric specifically designed to assess visual clue retrieval in reasoning contexts, and (2) a parameter-free, architecture-agnostic plugin that works across reasoning architectures (R1-OneVision, Ocean-R1, MM-Eureka). This contrasts with the matched notes’ approaches, which either assume structured knowledge graphs exist (Multimodal Knowledge Graph Construction) or require offline reconciliation phases (MultiRAG). The Mind-Map Agent’s real-time knowledge graph construction shares ClueTracer’s dynamic tracking philosophy, but ClueTracer’s focus on question-to-vision propagation is more targeted—it doesn’t require maintaining persistent graph structures, making it genuinely plug-and-play for any reasoning model.

Integration Potential

Interestingly, ClueTracer’s approach could enhance the frameworks described in the matched notes. For instance, integrating ClueTracer’s question-conditioned visual cue localization into Multimodal Knowledge Graph Construction could improve the contextualized visual entity extraction phase by ensuring entity candidates align with task-relevant visual regions rather than being uniformly extracted. Similarly, ClueTracer’s reasoning drift detection could serve as a validation layer within MultiRAG’s multi-source aggregation, flagging when intermediate reasoning has diverged from supporting evidence. The paper thus contributes a novel inference-time verification mechanism that treats hallucination suppression not as a knowledge problem (how to get better sources) but as a reasoning coherence problem (ensuring multi-step inference stays visually grounded).
