Answer First, Reason Later: Aligning Search Relevance via Mode-Balanced Reinforcement Learning

Source: arXiv:cs.LG Published: 2026-02-10 Rank: #5 (score: 0.98, max_sim: 0.82) Reading time: ~20 min Tags: RAG, LLM, RL, Search

Abstract

Building a search relevance model that achieves both low latency and high performance is a long-standing challenge in the search industry. To satisfy the millisecond-level response requirements of online systems while retaining the interpretable reasoning traces of Large Language Models (LLMs), we propose a novel \textbf{Answer-First, Reason Later (AFRL)} paradigm. This paradigm requires the model to output the definitive relevance score in the very first token, followed by a structured logical explanation. Inspired by the success of reasoning models, we adopt a “Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) + Reinforcement Learning (RL)” pipeline to achieve AFRL. However, directly applying existing RL training often leads to \textbf{mode collapse} in the search relevance task, where the model forgets complex long-tail rules in pursuit of high rewards. From an information theory perspective: RL inherently minimizes the \textbf{Reverse KL divergence}, which tends to seek probability peaks (mode-seeking) and is prone to “reward hacking.” On the other hand, SFT minimizes the \textbf{Forward KL divergence}, forcing the model to cover the data distribution (mode-covering) and effectively anchoring expert rules. Based on this insight, we propose a \textbf{Mode-Balanced Optimization} strategy, incorporating an SFT auxiliary loss into Stepwise-GRPO training to balance these two properties. Furthermore, we construct an automated instruction evolution system and a multi-stage curriculum to ensure expert-level data quality. Extensive experiments demonstrate that our 32B teacher model achieves state-of-the-art performance. Moreover, the AFRL architecture enables efficient knowledge distillation, successfully transferring expert-level logic to a 0.6B model, thereby reconciling reasoning depth with deployment latency.

Answer-First, Reason Later (AFRL) with Mode-Balanced Optimization

Core Idea: A search relevance paradigm that outputs the definitive relevance score in the first token followed by reasoning traces, trained with a hybrid RL+SFT approach that balances mode-seeking (reward maximization) and mode-covering (expert knowledge retention) to prevent mode collapse.

Problem It Solves: Traditional search relevance models face a latency-performance tradeoff: small models are fast but lack reasoning capability, while LLM-based models provide interpretable reasoning but are too slow for millisecond-level requirements. Standard RL training (e.g., GRPO) suffers from mode collapse where models forget complex long-tail relevance rules while pursuing high rewards.

How It Works:

  1. AFRL Architecture: Model is constrained to emit the relevance score as the very first output token, followed by structured logical explanation tokens
  2. Two-Stage Training: First apply Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) on expert-annotated relevance judgments with reasoning traces to establish baseline coverage of the data distribution
  3. Mode-Balanced GRPO: Augment Stepwise Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) with an auxiliary SFT loss term to balance reverse KL (mode-seeking, reward hacking prone) with forward KL (mode-covering, expert rule anchoring)
  4. Curriculum Learning: Use automated instruction evolution system to generate high-quality training data across multiple difficulty stages
  5. Knowledge Distillation: The AFRL structure (answer-first) enables efficient transfer from 32B teacher to 0.6B student model by focusing distillation on the critical first-token decision

Architecture:

    A[Query + Document] --> B[AFRL Model]
    B --> C[Token 1: Relevance Score]
    C --> D[Tokens 2-N: Reasoning Trace]
    D --> E[Structured Explanation]
    
    F[Mode-Balanced Training] --> B
    F --> G[GRPO Loss<br/>reverse KL]
    F --> H[SFT Auxiliary Loss<br/>forward KL]

Where It’s Useful:

  • Search relevance scoring systems requiring both sub-millisecond latency and interpretability
  • Any ranking task where first-token decisions enable early-exit optimizations
  • Production systems needing to distill large reasoning models into deployable small models
  • Scenarios with long-tail complex rules that must be preserved during RL optimization

Limitations / Caveats:

  • Requires high-quality expert-annotated reasoning traces for SFT initialization
  • The balance between SFT auxiliary loss weight and GRPO requires careful tuning to avoid undermining RL optimization
  • AFRL paradigm constrains model flexibility by forcing answer-first structure, which may not suit all reasoning tasks
  • Mode-balanced optimization adds computational overhead compared to pure RL training

Internal Use Cases:

  • RecSys ranking: Train recommendation models that output relevance scores instantly while providing interpretable reasoning for debugging and user trust
  • Search quality: Build latency-critical search rankers that maintain coverage of complex edge-case rules (e.g., temporal relevance, domain-specific jargon) during RL fine-tuning
  • Model compression: Use AFRL structure to distill reasoning capabilities from large teacher models into mobile-deployable rankers without losing explanation quality
  • RL stability: Apply mode-balanced optimization to any RL-based ranking pipeline prone to reward hacking or forgetting rare-but-important relevance patterns
