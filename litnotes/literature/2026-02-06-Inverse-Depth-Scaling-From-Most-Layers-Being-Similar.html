Inverse Depth Scaling From Most Layers Being Similar

Source: arXiv:cs.AI Published: 2026-02-05 Rank: #3 (score: 0.99, max_sim: 0.82) Reading time: ~15 min Tags: LLM, RAG

Abstract

Neural scaling laws relate loss to model size in large language models (LLMs), yet depth and width may contribute to performance differently, requiring more detailed studies. Here, we quantify how depth affects loss via analysis of LLMs and toy residual networks. We find loss scales inversely proportional to depth in LLMs, probably due to functionally similar layers reducing error through ensemble averaging rather than compositional learning or discretizing smooth dynamics. This regime is inefficient yet robust and may arise from the architectural bias of residual networks and target functions incompatible with smooth dynamics. The findings suggest that improving LLM efficiency may require architectural innovations to encourage compositional use of depth.

Key Techniques

  • Loss scaling with depth analysis
  • Ensemble averaging through redundant layers
  • Residual network architectural bias
  • Compositional learning inefficiency in LLMs

Inverse Depth Scaling Analysis

Core Idea: Loss in large language models scales inversely proportional to depth (L^-1) because most layers are functionally similar and reduce error through ensemble averaging rather than compositional learning.

Problem It Solves: Explains why simply adding more layers to LLMs yields diminishing returns and provides theoretical grounding for why current architectures are depth-inefficient, helping researchers understand where to focus architectural innovations.

How It Works:

  1. Empirically measure loss scaling as a function of depth across multiple LLM architectures
  2. Decompose residual network dynamics to isolate whether layers are learning compositionally (multiplicative depth benefits) or redundantly (averaging effects)
  3. Compare observed L^-1 scaling against theoretical predictions for smooth dynamics discretization (L^-2) and ensemble averaging (L^-1)
  4. Conclude that layer similarity dominates, meaning each layer makes similar corrections that average out noise rather than building hierarchical representations

Where It’s Useful:

  • Guiding architectural search for depth-efficient LLMs by identifying what prevents compositional learning
  • Predicting performance gains from scaling depth vs. width in resource-constrained settings
  • Explaining why very deep models don’t always outperform shallower ones with more parameters

Limitations / Caveats:

  • Analysis focuses on final loss scaling; doesn’t address learning dynamics during training
  • May not apply to architectures with explicit compositional structure (e.g., hierarchical transformers, mixture-of-depths)
  • Doesn’t provide prescriptive solutions for breaking out of the ensemble averaging regime

Internal Use Cases:

  • LLM Architecture Design: When scaling embedding models or ranking LLMs, prioritize width or architectural innovations (attention variants, sparse layers) over naively adding more transformer blocks
  • Model Compression: Prune middle layers aggressively since they likely provide redundant ensemble averaging rather than unique compositional features
  • Hybrid Architectures: Combine shallow LLMs with specialized deep modules for tasks requiring true hierarchical reasoning (multi-hop retrieval, complex query understanding)
