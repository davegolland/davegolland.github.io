MetaGen: Self-Evolving Roles and Topologies for Multi-Agent LLM Reasoning

Source: arXiv:cs.CL Published: 2026-01-27 Rank: #8 (score: 0.98, max_sim: 0.83) Reading time: ~20 min Tags: LLM, RAG, Search

Abstract

Large language models are increasingly deployed as multi-agent systems, where specialized roles communicate and collaborate through structured interactions to solve complex tasks that often exceed the capacity of a single agent. However, most existing systems still rely on a fixed role library and an execution-frozen interaction topology, a rigid design choice that frequently leads to task mismatch, prevents timely adaptation when new evidence emerges during reasoning, and further inflates inference cost. We introduce MetaGen, a training-free framework that adapts both the role space and the collaboration topology at inference time, without updating base model weights. MetaGen generates and rewrites query-conditioned role specifications to maintain a controllable dynamic role pool, then instantiates a constrained execution graph around a minimal backbone. During execution, it iteratively updates role prompts and adjusts structural decisions using lightweight feedback signals. Experiments on code generation and multi-step reasoning benchmarks show that MetaGen improves the accuracy and cost tradeoff over strong multi-agent baselines.

Key Techniques

  • Dynamic role generation
  • Adaptive collaboration topology
  • Query-conditioned role specifications
  • Iterative prompt and structure refinement

Analysis of MetaGen and Its Connection to Prior Work

MetaGen directly extends the “Agentic Reasoning Design Patterns” framework by operationalizing multi-agent collaboration—one of Ng’s identified “emerging technologies”—into a concrete, adaptive system. While Ng’s taxonomy positions multi-agent collaboration as a high-potential but nascent pattern, MetaGen demonstrates how to make it practical through two key innovations that address gaps in the original framework: query-conditioned role generation (moving beyond fixed agent identities) and dynamic topology adjustment (evolving the collaboration structure during execution rather than freezing it at design time). This builds on the reflection and tool-use patterns Ng outlined by embedding feedback loops not just within individual agents, but across the entire agent graph structure. The “lightweight feedback signals” MetaGen uses to iteratively update role prompts echo the reflection pattern’s self-critique mechanism, but extend it to the meta-level of agent composition itself—essentially making the multi-agent system reflect on and refine its own organizational structure.

The paper also provides a training-free implementation pathway for concepts explored in “Automated Multi-Agent Architecture Search” (ADAS/AFlow/EvoFlow). While ADAS frames multi-agent composition as a search problem over a discrete space of workflows and roles, MetaGen shifts from exhaustive search to inference-time generation and pruning. Instead of enumerating candidate configurations offline (as ADAS does with Monte Carlo Tree Search over graph topologies), MetaGen generates role specifications on-demand and constructs a “minimal backbone” execution graph dynamically. This is a crucial architectural difference: ADAS optimizes for task families through upfront search, while MetaGen optimizes for individual queries through runtime adaptation. Both reject static, hand-crafted topologies, but MetaGen’s approach addresses ADAS’s computational cost problem—evaluating every candidate configuration on full benchmarks—by using the query itself as a constraint that narrows the search space before execution begins. The “constrained execution graph” concept represents a middle ground between ADAS’s exhaustive supernet exploration and traditional fixed-topology systems.

Finally, MetaGen aligns philosophically with the “Self-Evolving Agents” survey’s vision of agents that transcend static designs, but focuses specifically on structural and compositional evolution rather than the survey’s broader scope (knowledge acquisition, learning mechanisms, autonomy). The survey identifies “inability to adapt to new tasks” as a fundamental LLM limitation; MetaGen tackles this through its “controllable dynamic role pool” that rewrites agent capabilities mid-inference. Where the self-evolving agents literature emphasizes long-term learning and memory mechanisms, MetaGen achieves adaptation through stateless, query-scoped generation—a more conservative but immediately deployable form of evolution. Its “training-free framework that adapts… without updating base model weights” directly addresses the survey’s concern about bottlenecks in knowledge transfer, demonstrating that meaningful agent evolution can occur at the prompt and topology level without gradient-based learning. This positions MetaGen as a pragmatic first step toward the survey’s ASI-oriented vision: self-modification through organizational plasticity rather than parametric updates.
