The Potential of CoT for Reasoning: A Closer Look at Trace Dynamics

Source: arXiv:cs.AI Published: 2026-02-16 Rank: #3 (score: 0.99, max_sim: 0.83) Reading time: ~30 min Tags: LLM

Abstract

Chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting is a de-facto standard technique to elicit reasoning-like responses from large language models (LLMs), allowing them to spell out individual steps before giving a final answer. While the resemblance to human-like reasoning is undeniable, the driving forces underpinning the success of CoT reasoning still remain largely unclear. In this work, we perform an in-depth analysis of CoT traces originating from competition-level mathematics questions, with the aim of better understanding how, and which parts of CoT actually contribute to the final answer. To this end, we introduce the notion of a potential, quantifying how much a given part of CoT increases the likelihood of a correct completion. Upon examination of reasoning traces through the lens of the potential, we identify surprising patterns including (1) its often strong non-monotonicity (due to reasoning tangents), (2) very sharp but sometimes tough to interpret spikes (reasoning insights and jumps) as well as (3) at times lucky guesses, where the model arrives at the correct answer without providing any relevant justifications before. While some of the behaviours of the potential are readily interpretable and align with human intuition (such as insights and tangents), others remain difficult to understand from a human perspective. To further quantify the reliance of LLMs on reasoning insights, we investigate the notion of CoT transferability, where we measure the potential of a weaker model under the partial CoT from another, stronger model. Indeed aligning with our previous results, we find that as little as 20% of partial CoT can “unlock” the performance of the weaker model on problems that were previously unsolvable for it, highlighting that a large part of the mechanics underpinning CoT are transferable.

CoT Potential Analysis

Core Idea: Assign a scalar potential score to each prefix of a chain-of-thought trace by measuring how much that prefix increases the probability of a correct final answer, enabling fine-grained attribution of which reasoning steps actually drive performance.

Problem It Solves: CoT prompting is treated as a monolithic technique, but it is unknown which parts of a reasoning trace contribute to correctness vs. which are irrelevant, misleading tangents, or lucky guesses — blocking principled improvement of reasoning systems.

How It Works:

  1. Define potential: For a partial CoT trace prefix (tokens 1..k), compute , i.e., the lift in correct-answer probability attributable to that prefix.
  2. Sample completions: Estimate by generating many completions from the model conditioned on the prefix and scoring final answers.
  3. Plot potential over trace length: Produce a potential curve across the full trace to identify where potential rises (insights), drops (tangents), or spikes abruptly (jumps/lucky guesses).
  4. CoT transferability test: Feed partial prefixes from a stronger model into a weaker model and measure the weaker model’s potential, quantifying how much reasoning “bootstrap” transfers across models.

Architecture (if applicable):

flowchart LR
    Q[Question] --> SM[Strong Model\nfull CoT trace]
    SM --> P["Prefix p_k\n(0%...100% of trace)"]
    P --> WM[Target Model\nsampled completions]
    WM --> S[Answer scoring]
    S --> PHI["Potential Φ(p_k)\nvs. trace position"]
    PHI --> C{Pattern\nClassification}
    C --> T[Tangent\nnon-monotone drop]
    C --> I[Insight\nsharp spike up]
    C --> LG[Lucky Guess\nspike near end]

Where It’s Useful:

  • Diagnosing reasoning quality in math/coding benchmarks (competition-level problems where ground-truth answers exist)
  • Selecting optimal CoT prefix length for speculative decoding or prompt caching
  • Cross-model distillation: determining how much of a strong model’s trace a weak model needs to “unlock” solvability

Limitations / Caveats:

  • Potential estimation requires many sampled completions per prefix, making it computationally expensive at scale
  • Requires access to ground-truth answers to score completions, limiting applicability to open-ended generation
  • “Lucky guess” and “insight” categories are identified post-hoc and resist mechanistic interpretation; the paper acknowledges some spikes remain opaque
  • Findings are on competition mathematics — generalization to other domains (code, science, etc.) is unverified

Internal Use Cases:

  • LLM reranking: Use potential curves to identify the minimal prefix length that separates good from bad reasoning paths, enabling early-exit reranking without full trace generation
  • Training data curation: Filter CoT training examples by removing traces with high tangent regions (potential drops) to improve SFT/RLHF data quality
  • Retrieval-augmented reasoning: Transferability finding (20% prefix unlocks weaker models) suggests injecting a short “insight prefix” from a retrieval system could substitute for expensive full CoT
