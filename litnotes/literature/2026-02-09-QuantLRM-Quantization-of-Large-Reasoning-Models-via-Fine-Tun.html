QuantLRM: Quantization of Large Reasoning Models via Fine-Tuning Signals

QuantLRM: Quantization of Large Reasoning Models via Fine-Tuning Signals

Abstract

Weight-only quantization is important for compressing Large Language Models (LLMs). Inspired by the spirit of classical magnitude pruning, we study whether the magnitude of weight updates during reasoning-incentivized fine-tuning can provide valuable signals for quantizing Large Reasoning Models (LRMs). We hypothesize that the smallest and largest weight updates during fine-tuning are more important than those of intermediate magnitude, a phenomenon we term “protecting both ends”. Upon hypothesis validation, we introduce QuantLRM, which stands for weight quantization of LRMs via fine-tuning signals. We fit simple restricted quadratic functions on weight updates to protect both ends. By multiplying the average quadratic values with the count of zero weight updates of channels, we compute channel importance that is more effective than using activation or second-order information. We run QuantLRM to quantize various fine-tuned models (including supervised, direct preference optimization, and reinforcement learning fine-tuning) over four reasoning benchmarks (AIME-120, FOLIO, temporal sequences, and GPQA-Diamond) and empirically find that QuantLRM delivers a consistent improvement for LRMs quantization, with an average improvement of 6.55% on a reinforcement learning fine-tuned model. Also supporting non-fine-tuned LRMs, QuantLRM gathers effective signals via pseudo-fine-tuning, which greatly enhances its applicability.

Key Techniques

  • Magnitude-based weight update analysis
  • Quadratic function fitting for importance scoring
  • Pseudo-fine-tuning for signal extraction

QuantLRM (Quantization of Large Reasoning Models via Fine-Tuning Signals)

Core Idea: Use the magnitude of weight updates during reasoning-incentivized fine-tuning as signals to identify which weights are important for quantization, protecting weights with both the smallest and largest update magnitudes.

Problem It Solves: Traditional weight-only quantization methods for LLMs don’t account for the unique properties of Large Reasoning Models (LRMs), where reasoning capabilities developed during fine-tuning may be lost when using generic importance metrics like activation statistics or second-order Hessian information.

How It Works:

  1. Collect fine-tuning signals: Track weight updates (Δw) during reasoning-focused fine-tuning (supervised fine-tuning, DPO, or RL fine-tuning) or simulate them via pseudo-fine-tuning for non-fine-tuned models
  2. Compute channel importance: Fit restricted quadratic functions on weight update magnitudes to identify “both ends” (smallest and largest updates), then multiply average quadratic values by the count of zero weight updates per channel
  3. Apply quantization: Use the computed channel importance scores to protect critical channels during weight-only quantization, preserving reasoning capabilities while compressing the model

Architecture:

flowchart LR
    A[Pre-trained LRM] --> B[Fine-tuning / Pseudo-FT]
    B --> C[Track Weight Updates Δw]
    C --> D[Fit Quadratic Function]
    D --> E[Compute Channel Importance]
    E --> F[Weight Quantization]
    F --> G[Compressed LRM]

Where It’s Useful:

  • Compressing reasoning models fine-tuned with RL (e.g., models trained with process reward models)
  • Quantizing instruction-tuned or DPO-aligned reasoning models for deployment
  • Reducing memory footprint of mathematical/logical reasoning models (AIME, GPQA benchmarks)
  • Edge deployment of reasoning-capable LLMs

Limitations / Caveats:

  • Requires access to fine-tuning data or ability to simulate pseudo-fine-tuning, adding computational overhead
  • The “protecting both ends” hypothesis may not generalize to all model architectures or reasoning task types
  • Quadratic function fitting introduces hyperparameters that may need task-specific tuning
  • Primarily validated on weight-only quantization; unclear if approach extends to activation quantization

Internal Use Cases:

  • Compressing reasoning-enhanced ranking models where multi-step inference is needed (e.g., query understanding with chain-of-thought)
  • Quantizing LLM-based feature extraction models that have been fine-tuned on domain-specific reasoning tasks
  • Deploying compressed reasoning models for real-time recommendation explanations while preserving logical coherence
  • Creating efficient student models via distillation where the teacher uses reasoning capabilities that must be preserved during compression
